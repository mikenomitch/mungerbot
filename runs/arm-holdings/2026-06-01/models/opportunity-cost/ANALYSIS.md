# Opportunity Cost — Arm Holdings (ARM)

**Discipline:** Economics  ·  **Intensity:** standard  ·  **As-of:** 2026-06-01

## What this model predicts

Munger's true filter is **relative, not absolute**: a dollar has no obligation to
be invested *here* — it can sit in a 3-month T-bill, an index fund, a known
compounder, or a cheaper same-theme peer. Every position is implicitly a *bet
against* the best foregone alternative. The question is never "is Arm a good
company?" (it is) but **"is buying Arm at $408.85 the BEST use of the next dollar
versus the obvious, lower-effort alternatives?"** Munger: *"Intelligent people
make decisions based on opportunity costs."*

If Arm clears the opportunity-cost bar, we should observe **at least one** of:
- (a) an expected forward return that **beats cash and the index by a margin wide
  enough to compensate for its far higher risk** (a hyper-cyclical, related-party-
  dependent, 86%-controlled equity should *demand* a return premium, not accept a
  discount);
- (b) economics or a moat **unavailable** in the alternatives — i.e. you cannot buy
  this quality of compounding elsewhere at a sane price;
- (c) a price that is **not** already discounting more than the alternatives are.

If instead Arm offers a **lower** prospective return than cash/index while carrying
**more** risk, the model fails hard: holding the alternative is strictly dominant.

## Evidence

**Supporting** (the bull's opportunity-cost argument — stated fairly):
- **Genuinely scarce asset.** ~99–100% of smartphone app processors and ~94% of
  automakers pay Arm; >20M developers; >325B cumulative chips — a software/ecosystem
  network effect you cannot replicate by buying an index. [DOSSIER §1, §4.1]
- **~97.5% gross margin, fortress net cash (~$3.1–3.2B), no bond wall** — a
  capital-light toll booth most index constituents cannot match. [DOSSIER §2.1, §2.3]
- **Real secular tailwind:** data-center share 11.5%→17.7% of total server-CPU units
  YoY; the royalty ladder (v8 ~2.5–3% → v9 ~5% → CSS ~10%+) raises take-rate on flat
  units. [DOSSIER §4.1–4.2] This is optionality an index dilutes away.

**Disconfirming** (cited — the opportunity-cost case against, which dominates):
- **Forward earnings yield BELOW the risk-free rate.** At ~163x forward P/E, Arm's
  forward earnings yield is **~0.6%** vs a **3-month T-bill at 3.68%** (2026-06-01).
  You are paid *less* to own a high-beta (β 3.78), forced-seller-exposed equity than
  to own a government bill. [DOSSIER §3.1, §7; TradingEconomics 3-mo bill 6/1/26]
- **~7x the index multiple for a worse risk profile.** Arm ~163x fwd P/E vs S&P 500
  **~22x** fwd P/E (~4.5% earnings yield, ~6–12% expected 2026 total return per
  Goldman). The index gives you mid-single-digit-plus returns diversified; Arm asks
  7x the multiple on one concentrated, cyclical name. [DOSSIER §3.1; Goldman Sachs
  2026 outlook; FactSet/MacroMicro fwd P/E ~21–22x]
- **~7x the multiple of the faster-growing same-theme peer.** Nvidia — *the very
  stock whose Computex launch doubled Arm* — trades at **~24x forward P/E** while
  growing data-center revenue **+92% YoY** ($215.9B FY26 revenue, +65%). If you want
  the AI-compute theme, the cleaner, faster-growing, more-liquid expression sits at
  ~1/7th Arm's multiple. [DOSSIER §3; NVIDIA Q1 FY27 8-K; GuruFocus NVDA fwd P/E
  25.3]
- **A known compounder is on sale at the same moment.** Berkshire ~14x trailing
  earnings, ~10% earnings yield, ~$397B cash optionality, trading ~36% *below* a DCF
  fair value. The archetypal "dollar of last resort" is cheaper than its own worth
  while Arm is ~74% *above* its bull-case worth. [24/7 Wall St 5/14/26; Simply Wall
  St; DOSSIER §3.3]
- **Negative expected return even if the bull thesis is RIGHT.** If Arm *hits* its
  aggressive FY2031 target (>$9 non-GAAP EPS), the 5-yr return from $408.85 is
  **−10.7%/yr at 25x exit, −7.4% at 30x, −1.9% at 40x, +2.5% at 50x**. To merely
  match an ~8% index return the market must still pay **~76x** earnings in 2031.
  [DOSSIER §3.2]
- **Priced above the consensus target.** Average sell-side PT **~$227–241** vs
  $408.85 close — even the bulls' own price implies ~−41% to −44%. The alternatives
  are *not* trading above their targets. [DOSSIER §3, §7]

**Independent research run for this model** (sources):
- 3-month T-bill **3.68%** on 2026-06-01 (the cash alternative's hurdle).
  [TradingEconomics, *US 3 Month Bill Yield*]
- S&P 500 forward P/E **~22x** / ~4.5% earnings yield; Goldman 2026 total-return

## Reasoning

**This model is highly relevant here** — arguably the single cleanest lens on the
Arm question — because Arm's *business quality is not the issue; its price relative
to alternatives is.* Opportunity cost is the discipline that converts "great
company" into "bad investment," and that conversion is exactly what the numbers do.

Run the four obvious alternatives Munger would actually weigh:

| Alternative | Forward yield / return | Risk | Verdict vs Arm |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cash (3-mo T-bill)** | 3.68% risk-free | ~none | **Dominates** — higher yield, zero permanent-loss risk |
| **S&P 500 index** | ~4.5% E-yield; ~6–12% '26 | diversified, moderate | **Dominates** — 7x lower multiple, no single-name tail |
| **Nvidia (same theme)** | ~4.2% E-yield; +92% DC growth | high but liquid, growing | **Dominates the *theme* trade** at ~1/7th the multiple |
| **Berkshire (compounder)** | ~10% E-yield; ~36% below DCF | low | **Dominates** as the "store of dollars" choice |
| **Arm @ $408.85** | **~0.6% fwd E-yield**; **negative 5-yr return even if bull case hits** | very high (β 3.78, 86% control, pledge, related-party, antitrust) | **Strictly dominated on every axis** |

The result is unusually one-sided: Arm offers the **lowest** prospective return of
the set while carrying the **highest** risk (forced-seller pledge mechanics,
30.5% related-party revenue that supplied the *majority* of FY26 growth, a live FTC
probe, a self-inflicted channel conflict, β 3.78, IV at the 100th percentile). The
model's failure condition — *lower return AND more risk than the alternative* — is
met against **all four** alternatives simultaneously. That is the textbook
definition of a strictly dominated choice.

**The "you can't buy this elsewhere" defense fails on price, not on quality.** It is
true the Arm ecosystem network effect is not purchasable via an index. But
opportunity cost is about the *price of the next dollar*, and the same secular AI-
compute exposure is available through Nvidia (faster-growing, ~1/7th the multiple)
or even cheaply through the broad semis complex. Scarcity of the *asset* does not
rescue a position when a near-substitute exposure trades at a fraction of the
implied expectation. Munger would say: *the moat is real, the price is insane, and a
real moat at an insane price is a worse bet than a good business at a fair price.*

**Steelmanning the bull (and where it breaks):** the only opportunity-cost case for
Arm requires the market to *keep* paying ~50–76x earnings in 2031 — i.e. the entry
multiple must persist for five years. But the alternatives don't need a permanent
re-rating to clear their (lower) bar; Arm does. Betting that today's +15.7%-momentum-
day, 2.7x-the-200-DMA multiple is *durable* is itself the opportunity-cost trap:
you're paying today for a future that, *even if it arrives*, returns less than cash.

**Check on our own bias (the model cuts both ways).** Opportunity cost can be abused
to reject *everything* ("cash always looks safe"). I tested the inversion: is cash/
index merely *recency-anchored* to look good while Arm's terminal value is being
under-imagined? No — the dossier's own bull ceiling (~$235 triangulated; ~$287 at
mgmt-$9 × 55x) sits **below** today's price, so even granting the optimistic
terminal value, the relative comparison holds. The conclusion is not "cash is
comfortable"; it is "the arithmetic of foregone return is decisively against Arm at
$408.85." Were Arm at, say, $130 (the dossier mid), this model's signal would flip
toward neutral/positive on a quality-adjusted basis. **The verdict is a price
verdict, not a business verdict** — which is precisely what the opportunity-cost
lens is built to deliver.

## Red flags

| Flag | Severity (low/med/high/fatal) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Forward earnings yield (~0.6%) **below** the risk-free T-bill (3.68%) | high | Negative risk-adjusted carry vs the simplest alternative; you are paid less than cash to bear extreme risk |
| Negative 5-yr return **even if the aggressive bull target is hit** | high | Opportunity cost is negative versus *every* alternative across plausible exit multiples; no positive-EV path without permanent ~50–76x re-rating |
| ~7x the index multiple and ~7x a faster-growing same-theme peer (NVDA) | high | A near-substitute AI-compute exposure exists at a fraction of the price — scarcity argument fails on price |
| Priced above the average sell-side target ($227–241 vs $409) | medium | The marginal-dollar comparison is unfavorable even on the bulls' own numbers |
| Entry multiple is transient (2.7x 200-DMA, +15.7% momentum day, IV 100th pct) | medium | The opportunity-cost gap could *widen* further if the multiple normalizes; mean-reversion risk is itself a foregone-return cost |

## What would flip this read (disconfirmers)

- **Price de-rates toward intrinsic value** (~$130 dossier mid, or even ~$200s):
  the forward earnings yield would rise above cash and the relative comparison would
  no longer be strictly dominated; signal moves toward neutral/positive.
- **Alternatives become unattractive in tandem:** a cash rate falling toward zero
  *and* the index/Nvidia re-rating to Arm-like multiples would shrink the
  opportunity-cost gap (low probability at the as-of date).
- **Earnings step-change that collapses the multiple at a constant price:** e.g.
  AGI-CPU revenue + royalty acceleration drives non-GAAP EPS toward the ~$22 needed
  for a 30x multiple at today's price by ~2031 — a ~2.5x beat of the bull target.
  Possible but unprecedented at this scale; would require the supply-capped AGI CPU
  and related-party revenue to convert into durable, arm's-length owner cash flow.
- **Demonstrated durable ~50%+ owner-FCF compounding** (not non-GAAP EPS) that makes
  the reverse-DCF hurdle (~35–40% FCF CAGR for 15 yrs) look conservative rather than
  fanciful.

**Bottom line:** Against the four obvious alternatives Munger would actually weigh —
cash (3.68% risk-free), the index (~22x, ~6–12% expected), Nvidia (~24x, +92% data-
center growth), and a cheap known compounder (Berkshire ~14x, ~36% below DCF) — Arm
at $408.85 is **strictly dominated**: it offers the *lowest* prospective return
(~0.6% forward earnings yield, below T-bills; **negative** even if the bull target is
hit) while carrying by far the *highest* risk. The business is genuinely excellent
and the moat is unpurchaseable in an index — but opportunity cost judges the price of
the *next dollar*, and that dollar earns more, more safely, almost anywhere else.
This is a textbook PASS on relative grounds; the read flips only if the price
de-rates toward intrinsic value (~$130–200s).
