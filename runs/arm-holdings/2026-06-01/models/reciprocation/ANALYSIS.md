# Reciprocation Tendency — Arm Holdings (ARM)

**Discipline:** Psychology  ·  **Intensity:** lens  ·  **As-of:** 2026-06-01

## What this model predicts

Munger's reciprocation tendency: humans (and the organizations they run) feel a deep
compulsion to repay favors, gifts, and concessions in kind — and this lever is routinely
*weaponized* commercially. The dark side is that a stream of reciprocal favors can prop up
reported economics that would not survive an arm's-length market test. As a lens over a
business, the model asks: **are the numbers being held up by reciprocity rather than by
genuine, willing-buyer demand?** It names three concrete vectors:

1. **Channel kickbacks** — paying (overtly or covertly) for distribution/placement, or
   booking revenue from a channel you secretly fund, so "sales" are partly a recycled favor.
2. **Sweetheart supplier / counterparty deals** — related parties or favored partners
   transacting at non-market prices, where the "favor" (above-market pricing, prepayment,
   guaranteed volume) flows because of the relationship, not the merits.
3. **Regulatory capture** — a reciprocal relationship with regulators/policymakers (revolving
   doors, lobbying, favorable rule-making) that protects the moat or suppresses competition.

**If this model fires *against* Arm, we should observe:** a material slice of revenue from
related/controlled parties at prices that are *not* market-set; revenue recognized but not
yet collected (a favor extended on payment terms); growth concentrated in exactly those
reciprocal buckets; circular relationships where Arm's customers are also funded by Arm's
controller; and the *absence* of a true independent check (a controlled board that "approves"
related-party terms it cannot actually contest). And — applying the lens to *ourselves and the
market* — we should ask whether the **bull narrative** is being inflated by reciprocal hype
(partners publicly endorsing each other's chips in a tightly-coupled AI-capex web).

The model also has a *favorable* read worth testing: reciprocity is the glue of a healthy
ecosystem (Arm gives developers free tools/reference designs; they "repay" with lock-in). That
is benign, even moat-building. The job is to separate benign ecosystem reciprocity from
**revenue-propping** reciprocity.

## Evidence

**Supporting (reciprocity is propping the numbers) — cited:**

- **30.5% of FY2026 revenue is related-party** — $1,499M, **+82% YoY** vs +7% for external
  customers, i.e. **the majority of reported growth came from non-arm's-length parties.**
  [DOSSIER §8 #2; 20-F FY2026 Notes 4 & 20; `artifacts/filings-related-party-revenue.csv`]
- **The SoftBank "Consulting Agreement": $704.4M in FY2026, up +384% YoY** (from $145.5M), of
  which **~$645.8M is an UNBILLED contract asset** — revenue *recognized* but not yet billed or
  collected, owed by Arm's own ~86.4% controller. This single line was the **biggest driver of
  FY26 license-revenue growth.** [`filings-related-party-revenue.csv`; DOSSIER §8 #2]
  Independent confirmation of *what* this agreement is: Arm provides SoftBank "certain technical
  consultancy and advisory services relating to potential transactions, strategic partnerships,
  [and] licensing arrangements." [Arm F-1/424B4, SEC EDGAR, 2023] — i.e. the parent is paying
  the subsidiary for *advisory work*, the textbook shape of a related-party transfer whose
  price is set by relationship, not a market.
- **Arm China IPLA: $790.6M (~16% of revenue)** from an entity Arm holds only **~4.8% indirect
  economic interest** in, that **"neither we nor SoftBank control,"** and where **Arm relies on
  Arm-China-self-reported royalty data** (credit-loss allowance up to $28.3M). The #1 single
  customer is a deconsolidated, self-reporting related party. [DOSSIER §4.4, §8 #2; Note 20]
- **The board cannot police any of this.** SoftBank owns ~86.4%, the board is **not
  majority-independent** (Nasdaq controlled-company exemption), Masayoshi Son chairs *and* runs
  the controller, and the Shareholder Governance Agreement gives SoftBank **consent rights over
  auditor changes and material accounting-policy changes.** The related-party pricing is governed
  by "SoftBank's related-party process, **not** market negotiation." [DOSSIER §6, §9 gap #1]
- **Circular AI-capex reciprocity.** Arm's AGI-CPU "lead customer/launch partners" include
  **OpenAI**, while SoftBank (Arm's controller) co-founded the **$500B Stargate** JV with OpenAI
  and reportedly committed **~$22.5B to OpenAI**, and bought **Ampere ($6.5B)** cashing out Arm's
  own stake. [DOSSIER §6.1; news lane; about.fb.com 2026-03-24] The same controller funds Arm's
  customers and counterparties — a web in which "demand" can be partly the controller's own
  capital recycled back as Arm bookings. (Judgment: the AGI-CPU *partner commitments* themselves
  are disclosed as co-development/volume commitments with **no disclosed kickback economics** —
  so this is a *circularity-of-funding* flag, not a proven channel kickback.)
- **The Critical Audit Matter is revenue recognition on long-term contracts** (performance-
  obligation ID, standalone-selling-price estimation) — precisely the judgment areas where a
  related-party "favor" on pricing/timing would hide. [DOSSIER §6 controls]

**Disconfirming (reciprocity is benign / not propping the numbers) — cited:**

- **The auditor signed off; no restatement; no current material weakness.** Deloitte issued a
  clean opinion; disclosure controls effective 2026-03-31; the prior (FY21–22) material weakness
  was remediated in FY25; clawback box unchecked. The related-party items are **fully disclosed**
  in Notes 4 & 20 — this is visible reciprocity, not hidden fraud. [DOSSIER §6, §2.6]
- **The core franchise is NOT reciprocity-dependent.** Arm taxes **~99% of smartphone app
  processors and ~94% of automakers** because its ISA is the genuine industry standard with
  >20M developers and 325B+ cumulative chips — willing buyers paying because they must, not as a
  favor. The benign "ecosystem reciprocity" (free tools → developer lock-in) is moat-building,
  not number-propping. [DOSSIER §1, §4]
- **No evidence of classic channel kickbacks.** Arm is an IP licensor / (now) chip supplier with
  **no distributor channel to bribe**; there is no disclosed "buy-our-IP-and-we'll-pay-you"
  scheme. Royalties are pulled by the chipmakers' own end-demand. [DOSSIER §1; judgment]
- **No evidence of regulatory capture — the opposite.** Far from a captured regulator shielding
  Arm, Arm faces a **live, hostile FTC probe (opened 2026-05-15)**, plus EU and Korea KFTC
  actions, examining whether Arm *abuses* its position. Whatever Arm's lobbying, it is **losing
  the regulatory reciprocity game**, not winning it. [DOSSIER §8 #5]
- **Even ex-related-party, Arm still grew +7% and is a real ~$3.4B external-revenue business.**
  Strip out 100% of the related-party bucket and a profitable, high-gross-margin franchise
  remains. The reciprocity question is about *quality and growth attribution*, not about a
  hollow shell. [DOSSIER §8 #2; judgment]

**Independent lens research (cited):** Confirmed the AGI-CPU partner roster (Meta lead; OpenAI,
Cerebras, Cloudflare, F5, SAP, SK Telecom, Positron, Rebellions as launch partners) and that
these are framed as **multi-generation co-development / commercial commitments** with no
disclosed reciprocal payment terms. [Meta Newsroom 2026-03-24, about.fb.com; Tom's Hardware
2026-03-25; DCD 2026-03-25] Confirmed the SoftBank consulting agreement is an advisory-services
arrangement (not a product sale). [Arm F-1, SEC EDGAR, 2023]

## Reasoning

**On the subject (Arm/SoftBank).** This is one of the *highest-relevance* companies the
reciprocation lens will see in this run. The mandate's middle vector — **sweetheart
related-party deals** — is not hypothetical here; it is the dominant feature of the FY2026
growth story. The structure is textbook: a ~86%-controlling parent that (a) routes a fast-growing
**$704M "consulting" stream** to its subsidiary, **~$646M of it unbilled**, (b) sits on both
sides of the table via a non-independent board and consent rights over the *auditor* and
*accounting policy*, and (c) simultaneously deploys tens of billions into the AI-capex ecosystem
(OpenAI/Stargate/Ampere) that constitutes Arm's own customer base. When the controller can decide
what the subsidiary gets paid for "advice," can influence how that revenue is recognized, and is
also funding the subsidiary's customers, **the price is set by relationship, not by a market** —
which is exactly the condition under which reciprocation props the reported number. The fact that
the related-party bucket grew **+82% while external demand grew +7%** is the single most damning
data point: the *marginal* dollar of FY26 growth was disproportionately a favor from inside the
house, not a willing payment from outside it.

Crucially, **the normal disconfirmer for a related-party flag — an independent board and an
arm's-length pricing process — is absent by design.** Munger's reciprocation tendency is most
dangerous precisely when there is no countervailing check, because the favor-exchange runs
unchecked. Here the controlled-company structure removes the check. That does **not** prove the
prices are off-market (I have no market comparable for "SoftBank strategic-advisory services"),
but it removes my ability to *rule out* that they are, and it removes minority shareholders'
ability to contest them. For an earnings-quality / reciprocation lens, an un-checkable
related-party stream that is ~$0.65B unbilled and supplied the majority of growth is a
**negative signal of high strength**.

**On channel kickbacks and regulatory capture specifically:** these two of the three named
vectors **do not fire**. There is no distribution channel to kick back to, and Arm is the
*target* of regulators, not their beneficiary — if anything Arm is on the wrong end of the
reciprocity ledger with the state. So I down-weight the "regulatory capture propping numbers"
thesis to roughly zero; the live FTC/EU/KFTC actions are a risk, but they are the *inverse* of
capture. This is why the signal is **negative but not fatal**: only one of three vectors is hot.

**On our own analytical bias (the lens turned inward).** Reciprocation also predicts that *the
market's* enthusiasm may be reciprocally inflated. Arm's stock **doubled in two weeks on a
*partner's* (Nvidia's) product launch, not on Arm's own guidance** (management declined to
raise), and the bull case leans on a chorus of mutually-endorsing AI partners
(Nvidia<->Arm<->Meta<->OpenAI<->SoftBank) who each benefit from the others' narrative. That is a
reciprocity-of-hype dynamic — partners "repaying" each other with public commitments and
co-marketing — and it should make us *discount*, not credit, the partner-endorsement evidence.
The honest internal check cuts the other way too: I must not let the vivid related-party flag
make me *over*-attribute the whole business to reciprocity. The core ~99%-smartphone toll is real
and willing-buyer-driven; the reciprocation problem is concentrated in the **growth and
earnings-quality margin**, not the franchise core. I have weighted it as a quality/attribution
problem, not an existential one.

**Relevance vs strength vs conviction.** Relevance is high (4) — related-party reciprocity is
front-and-center. Strength is high (4) — the evidence (30.5% of revenue, +82% growth, $646M
unbilled, no independent check) is concrete and primary-sourced. Conviction is moderate (3): I am
confident the *structure* invites reciprocity-propping and that an independent check is absent,
but I **cannot prove** the prices are off-market (no comparable), and the auditor's clean opinion
plus full disclosure are genuine mitigants. This is a strong negative *quality* signal, not a
proven fraud.

## What would flip this read (disconfirmers)

- **Disclosure that related-party pricing is independently benchmarked / arm's-length** (e.g., an
  audit-committee fairness review, a transfer-pricing study, or third-party comparables) — would
  defuse the "favor not market price" core of the flag.
- **The $646M unbilled SoftBank contract asset gets billed and *collected* on normal terms** in
  FY27 — would show it was real, willing-buyer revenue, not a soft favor parked as a receivable.
- **External (non-related-party) revenue re-accelerates** so growth no longer depends on the
  related-party bucket — would show the franchise stands without the favor stream.
- **An independent board / minority-protective mechanism is installed** (SoftBank reduces control,
  or genuine independent directors gain veto over related-party terms) — restores the check.
- **Arm China brought back into a controlled, audited reporting line** (no longer self-reported) —
  removes the largest self-report reciprocity risk.
- Conversely, the read would **harden** if the unbilled consulting balance keeps growing without
  cash collection, or if the related-party share of revenue rises further.

**Bottom line:** Of the model's three vectors, **sweetheart related-party deals fire hard** while
channel kickbacks and regulatory capture do not (Arm has no channel to bribe and is the *target*
of regulators, not their pet). The franchise core is willing-buyer demand, but the **FY2026
growth and earnings quality are materially propped by reciprocity from inside the house**: a
$704M SoftBank "consulting" stream (~$646M unbilled, +384%), a self-reporting Arm China #1
customer, and 30.5% of revenue / the *majority* of growth coming from related parties — all
under a board with no power to contest the terms and a controller that simultaneously funds Arm's
own AI customers. Turned inward, the lens also says the market's two-week doubling is partly
reciprocal hype to be discounted. Net: a clear **negative quality signal**, not fatal in
isolation, that should sharpen the earnings-quality, incentives, and governance reads.
