# Use-It-or-Lose-It — Arm Holdings (ARM)

**Discipline:** Psychology  ·  **Intensity:** lens  ·  **As-of:** 2026-06-01

## What this model predicts

Munger's "use it or lose it" tendency: a skill, a muscle, an institutional
competence decays when it isn't exercised. Applied to a company, the lens asks
whether the firm is **coasting on legacy strength while its core engine — R&D,
engineering talent, distribution/ecosystem — quietly atrophies.** Decay rarely
shows up in the current P&L; it shows up first in inputs and leading indicators,
then in product, then (years later) in revenue.

**If Arm's core competence were atrophying, we should observe:**
1. **R&D shrinking or flat** in absolute terms and as a share of revenue; R&D
   spend not translating into new shippable capability (a "maintenance-mode"
   roadmap that refreshes legacy rather than extends the frontier).
2. **Engineering talent bleeding out** — net outflow of senior architects, rising
   attrition, key-person departures to rivals/startups, inability to staff new
   initiatives, weak employer-brand signal.
3. **Distribution / ecosystem muscle weakening** — the developer base and
   design-win pipeline stalling; switching costs eroding because Arm stopped
   investing in the toolchain/software moat.
4. **A "rest-and-vest" cultural tell** — comp structured to reward tenure and
   share-price beta rather than output; complacency in employee sentiment.

Conversely, if Arm is **using** its competence hard, we should see R&D rising and
*productive* (new IP shipping, the royalty ladder climbing), talent flowing **in**
(not out), the ecosystem expanding, and effort being *reallocated* (pruning
non-core) rather than left to rot. The honest twist this lens forces: even a firm
exercising its muscle can **mis-direct** it — building a brand-new capability it
let lapse for decades — which is a different failure mode worth flagging.

## Evidence

**Disconfirming the atrophy thesis** (i.e. evidence the muscle is being USED — the
dominant body of evidence):

- **R&D is the largest and fastest-growing cost line, not a withering one.** R&D
  rose to **$2,776M in FY2026 (+34% YoY), now 56% of revenue** (from $1,979M FY24,
  $2,071M FY25). [DOSSIER §2.1] This is the opposite of maintenance-mode spend.
- **Engineering headcount is expanding fast.** Total employees **8,330 in 2025,
  +17.4% YoY** (and +19% the prior year). [Macrotrends/Glassdoor via web search,
  2026] Arm is *hiring into* its core, not shrinking it.
- **Arm stood up an entirely new silicon-engineering organization from scratch.**
  For the AGI CPU, "a once-tiny team has grown to over **1,000 people**," with
  **$71M** spent on three new Austin lab rooms over ~18 months and a **~$500M**
  program budget. [CNBC 2026-03-24; TechCrunch 2026-03-24; mlq.ai] You do not
  build a 1,000-engineer team and a $71M lab if your competence is atrophying.
- **Talent flow is INBOUND.** Reuters/Tom's Hardware: "Arm is actively poaching
  talent from its rivals" to build its own processors. [Tom's Hardware 2026, citing
  Reuters] Arm is a net *acquirer* of chip talent — the classic anti-atrophy signal.
  (SoftBank's $6.5B Ampere acquisition, whose engineering team is "widely believed
  to be contributing to future generations," further deepens, not drains, the bench.
  [mlq.ai; DOSSIER §6.1])
- **The R&D is demonstrably PRODUCTIVE — the muscle produces output.** The royalty
  ladder is climbing exactly as designed: **Armv9 ~25% of royalties and on track to
  a 60–70% attach target; CSS ~20% of royalties and projected toward ~50%**, with a
  CSS license ~2x an equivalent v9 core. [Benzinga 2025-02; TIKR 2026; DOSSIER §4.2]
  New shippable IP keeps arriving: **Neoverse V3 (Poseidon, +84% AI uplift vs V2),
  N3 (+196% sim), SME2/KleidiAI** for on-device GenAI, and the AGI CPU itself.
  [DCD; Arm Newsroom; nextplatform] This is a firm extending the frontier.
- **Ecosystem/distribution muscle is intact and growing.** >20M developers, >325B
  cumulative chips, "~50% of compute shipped to top hyperscalers in 2025 is
  Arm-based; >1B Neoverse cores deployed." [DOSSIER §1, §4.1] The software/ecosystem
  network effect — the load-bearing moat — is being actively reinforced, not coasted.
- **Deliberate pruning of NON-core, not decay of core.** Arm spun off the IoT
  software business (Pelion); "almost all of the management in the US were laid off
  in early December." [TheLayoff.com via web search; DOSSIER §6 notes the pre-IPO
  IoT-group walk-back] Shedding a non-core, lower-return unit to concentrate effort
  on CPU IP and data center is the *opposite* of letting core skills rot.
- **No "rest-and-vest" cultural tell in the data.** Glassdoor: comp/benefits **4.4/5**,
  overall **4.5/5 (89% recommend)**, CEO approval **93%**. [DOSSIER §6; Glassdoor
  via web search] Industry engineering attrition is benign (~12%, flat YoY) and no
  Arm-specific spike surfaced. RSU/ESPP packages (15% discount, 2-yr lookback, 6%
  401k match) are *retention* tools.

**Supporting the atrophy / mis-direction thesis** (the honest other side — these are
the real Use-It-or-Lose-It risks here, though most are "contested/mis-directed
competence," not classic decay):

- **The competence Arm must now USE in merchant silicon is one it let LAPSE for ~35
  years.** Building/selling a finished chip is "a new business for Arm" (CEO Haas);
  "Arm AGI CPU" appears 30x in the FY2026 20-F vs **ZERO** the year before. [DOSSIER
  §1] By the very logic of this model, a muscle unused since 1990 is *weak* — Arm is
  re-learning, not exercising, fab/product/supply-chain skills. Execution evidence is
  thin: the AGI CPU is **supply-capped** (capacity for ~$1B of ~$2B demand), the
  stock fell ~7–10% on record results because management **couldn't secure foundry
  capacity**, and unit economics/inventory model are undisclosed. [DOSSIER §1, §8.7]
  This is the model's strongest live concern — not that the core is rotting, but that
  R&D muscle and ~$545M capex (11% of revenue, up from 1–3%) are being **diverted**
  into a capability Arm has to rebuild from a standing start. [DOSSIER §2.2]
- **The talent that IS leaving the broader Arm-ecosystem orbit flows to RISC-V.**
  Meta acquired RISC-V CPU startup **Rivos**; Qualcomm acquired **Ventana**; ex-Intel
  architects founded RISC-V startups (Oxmiq). [web search; DOSSIER §4.3] The
  *competence* of designing Arm-class cores is being contested by an open, royalty-free
  substitute — a "lose it" risk to Arm's pricing power if the ecosystem's best
  engineers increasingly build the alternative. (This is competition pressure on the
  moat more than atrophy of Arm's own bench — but the model flags it.)
- **R&D productivity is assumed to IMPROVE, not yet proven to.** The bull math
  (45.9% net margin) **requires R&D growth to slow** relative to revenue. [TIKR 2026]
  Today R&D is *out-growing* revenue (+34% vs +23%) and operating margin is falling
  (GAAP 20.7%->18.3%; non-GAAP op margin 46.7%->43.0%). [DOSSIER §2.1] So far the
  muscle is being worked harder for *declining* incremental return — a soft early
  signal worth monitoring, though attributable to deliberate front-loaded investment.
- **SoftBank control + heavy SBC could, over time, breed complacency.** ~86.4% control,
  no real minority check, ~$1B+/yr SBC, comp milestones tied to **market-cap/share-price**
  ($1T/$1.5T/$2T) rather than per-share cash generation. [DOSSIER §6] No *current*
  evidence of rest-and-vest, but the incentive architecture rewards share-price beta —
  a structural condition under which a muscle *could* be allowed to soften later.

## Reasoning

This lens is most useful when a dominant incumbent is quietly milking a franchise.
Arm is **not** that case today — and that is itself a clean, decision-useful read.
On every primary axis the model tests — R&D intensity, talent flow, ecosystem
investment, reallocation discipline — Arm is **exercising** its core competence
hard, arguably to a fault. R&D is 56% of revenue and rising; the firm built a
1,000-engineer silicon team and a $71M lab from nothing; it is *poaching* talent
rather than donating it; the royalty ladder (v9, CSS) shows the R&D is converting
into higher-value IP; and it pruned a non-core IoT unit to concentrate effort. A
company whose muscle is atrophying does none of these things. **Signal: positive
for the model's core question (no atrophy).**

The honest re-frame the discipline forces: the genuine Use-It-or-Lose-It risk at
Arm is not *decay* but **mis-direction**. Arm is now spending its scarce engineering
muscle (and abandoning its capital-light economics — capex 11% of revenue, owner-FCF
~zero for three years per DOSSIER §2.2) to **rebuild a finished-silicon competence it
deliberately let lapse for 35 years**. By this model's own logic, a long-unused muscle
is weak: the supply-cap stumble and undisclosed unit economics are exactly the
"re-learning curve" symptoms you'd predict. That is a real risk — but it is a strategy
and execution risk surfaced *adjacent* to this lens, already owned by the moat,
inversion, and capital-allocation models; it is not evidence that Arm's *core* CPU-IP
competence is rotting. If anything the core is being reinforced (Neoverse, SME2,
ecosystem) while a *new* muscle is being built alongside it.

**Check on our own analytical bias (the model applied to us).** Use-it-or-lose-it
cuts at the analyst too: it is tempting to *reach* for an atrophy narrative because
the stock is grotesquely expensive (~231x non-GAAP, MoS ~ -68% at midpoint, DOSSIER
§3) and a "they're coasting" story would conveniently reinforce the bear case. I must
not let valuation revulsion launder itself into a fundamental-decay finding the
evidence does not support. The data says the opposite of atrophy. The right
conclusion is that **this lens does not contribute to the bear case** — overvaluation
and SoftBank/related-party/antitrust risk are the binding constraints (other models'
turf), and pretending the engine is decaying would be intellectually dishonest.
Equally, I should not let Arm's impressive activity *budget* be mistaken for proven
*productivity* — spending $500M and 1,000 engineers is using the muscle, but the
return on that effort (margins falling, AGI-CPU economics unknown) is not yet
demonstrated. Net: positive signal, moderate strength, and explicitly **not** a fatal
or even materially negative input.

**Relevance is moderate (3/5).** The lens is highly applicable to *mature, milking*
incumbents; Arm is the inverse — a company in aggressive build mode — so the model's
sharpest edge (catching hidden decay) finds little to bite. A clear "no atrophy" is a
genuine signal, but synthesis should down-weight this lens relative to inversion,
incentives, valuation, and moat, which carry the load on Arm.

## What would flip this read (disconfirmers)

- **R&D flattening or falling** as a share of revenue without a commensurate margin
  recovery, signaling a shift into milk-the-franchise mode.
- **Net outflow of senior architects / a key-person exodus** (e.g. Chief Architect or
  core Neoverse/ISA leads departing to RISC-V ventures), or attrition spiking above
  industry norms — would convert "talent flows in" to "talent bleeds out."
- **A stalling roadmap**: v9/CSS attach rates plateauing well below the 60–70% / ~50%
  targets, or two-plus product cycles of mere legacy refresh with no new capability —
  the muscle worked but producing nothing new.
- **Ecosystem erosion**: developer count or hyperscaler-deployment share declining;
  RISC-V displacing Arm in *general-purpose servers or smartphones* (not just MCU/IoT)
  — evidence the distribution moat was under-invested and is decaying.
- **The AGI-CPU effort visibly cannibalizing the core**: core CPU-IP releases slipping
  or quality regressing because engineering was diverted to silicon — the mis-direction
  risk turning into actual core atrophy.

**Bottom line:** Arm fails the atrophy test in the right direction: R&D is 56% of
revenue and growing 34%, headcount is up 17%, it built a 1,000-engineer silicon team
and a $71M lab from scratch, it is *poaching* talent rather than losing it, and the
royalty ladder (v9, CSS) shows R&D converting into higher-value IP — this is a
company exercising its core competence hard, not coasting on legacy strength. The one
legitimate Use-It-or-Lose-It concern is **mis-direction, not decay**: Arm is spending
that muscle (and its capital-light economics) to rebuild a finished-silicon capability
it let lapse for 35 years, with the supply-cap stumble and undisclosed economics as
classic re-learning symptoms — but that is execution/strategy risk owned by the moat
and inversion lenses. From *this* model's vantage there is no fundamental-decay case;
the bear thesis on Arm must rest on valuation and SoftBank/related-party/antitrust
risk, not on a rotting engine.
