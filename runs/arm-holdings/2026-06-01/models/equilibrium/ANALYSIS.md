# Equilibrium — Arm Holdings (ARM)

**Discipline:** Physics  ·  **Intensity:** lens  ·  **As-of:** 2026-06-01

## What this model predicts

Equilibrium is the physicist's intuition that systems settle toward a stable, low-energy state, and the economist's corollary that **super-normal returns are a temporary disequilibrium**: where one actor earns far above the cost of capital, capital, substitutes, and counter-pressure flow toward that pool until returns are competed back down toward the system's long-run resting level. The lens does not ask "is the moat real?" — it asks "what is the *attractor*, and how far is today's snapshot from it?"

Applied to Arm, the model makes specific, testable predictions:

1. **The ~98% IP gross margin is a thermodynamic anomaly that must be defended, not assumed.** A near-frictionless 98%-margin toll on ~99% of a global market is the maximal-energy state. The model predicts forces — a free substitute (RISC-V), buyer counter-pressure (in-housing, litigation), and regulators — are continuously pushing it down. If Arm is *near* equilibrium, royalty rates and the realized take-rate should be **near a ceiling**, not freely expandable.
2. **The royalty ladder (v8 ~2.5% → v9 ~5% → CSS ~10%+) is the disequilibrium engine, and it has a hard ceiling.** Every extra point of chip-ASP capture raises the prize for a customer to defect. The model predicts the ladder asymptotes: the marginal chip's take-rate converges to the price of the best free/in-house alternative *plus* a switching-cost premium that itself erodes as RISC-V tooling matures.
3. **The AGI-CPU pivot is a move from an anomalous-margin state into a *normal-margin* state.** Merchant data-center silicon has a well-known equilibrium: ~40-50% gross, ~25-30% operating. The model predicts mix-shift toward silicon **mechanically drags blended margins toward that lower attractor**, regardless of execution skill — you cannot out-execute thermodynamics; a 40%-margin dollar dilutes a 98%-margin book.
4. **ROIC reverts toward WACC.** If Arm is approaching competitive equilibrium, the spread between returns on capital and the cost of capital should be *thin and narrowing*, not 30%+. The model predicts ROIC drifts toward ~10-12% (WACC), not the 30%+ a wide-moat compounder sustains.
5. **The price embeds the *opposite* of equilibrium.** A reverse-DCF at the current price requires returns to *accelerate and stay accelerated for 15 years* — i.e. it prices a permanent, widening disequilibrium. The model predicts mean-reversion of *both* the operating economics *and* the multiple.

**If this model applies favorably to a long thesis,** we should see returns *rising* and a *widening* moat with no credible reversion force. **If it applies unfavorably,** we should see take-rate near a ceiling, a free substitute gaining, a margin-diluting mix shift underway, ROIC already thin vs WACC, and a price that has run far ahead of any stable resting state.

## Evidence

**Supporting the disequilibrium / reversion read (cited):**

- **ROIC is already only a thin spread over WACC, and falling.** Clean ROIC ~13.7% (ROIC-incl-cash ~8%) vs WACC ~10.5%; ROE 12.0% "and falling"; ROA 10.8%→7.2% YoY. The dossier's own key judgment: *"clean ROIC ~13-15% vs ~10% WACC is only a thin positive spread — NOT wide-moat 30%+ economics."* This is precisely what a system *near* equilibrium looks like — not what a system earning a durable, widening rent looks like. [DOSSIER §2.4]
- **Margins are visibly mean-reverting downward despite +23% revenue.** GAAP operating margin 23-25% (FY22-23) → 20.7% (FY25) → 18.3% (FY26); non-GAAP operating margin 46.7%→43.0% YoY. R&D is now 56% of revenue and *outgrowing* revenue. The energy required to *hold position* is rising. [DOSSIER §2.1]
- **The AGI-CPU pivot moves Arm into a lower-margin attractor.** Management is moving from a ">95%-gross-margin IP licensor to 40-50%-margin merchant silicon," with capex inflecting to 11% of revenue (was 1-3%) and opex pressuring operating income for ~8 quarters (Morgan Stanley OW→EW, 2026-04-07). [DOSSIER §8 item 6, §2.2]
- **The merchant-CPU equilibrium is observable in the incumbents.** Intel gross margin ~39.4% (Q1'26), data-center operating margin ~30.5%; AMD ~50% gross, ~27.7% data-center operating margin (Q1'25/Q1'26). [my research: SEC Intel 8-K Q1 FY2026; tweaktown/Daloopa 2026] This is the resting state Arm's silicon revenue reverts toward — roughly **half** the IP gross margin. You cannot mix a growing 40-50%-margin business into a 98%-margin book without dragging the blend down.
- **A free substitute exists and is gaining at the low end — the classic equilibrium-forcing mechanism.** RISC-V is royalty-free, "winning in MCU/IoT and increasingly auto-MCUs," backed by Arm's own largest customers (Qualcomm, Google, Meta, Nvidia), most dangerous in Arm's ~19%-revenue PRC market; Qualcomm bought RISC-V designer Ventana (Dec-2025) as an explicit Arm hedge. A free alternative is the textbook arbitrageur that competes a rent down. [DOSSIER §4.3]
- **Buyer counter-pressure is proven, not theoretical.** Arm *lost* the Qualcomm/Nuvia license fight (court granted Qualcomm JMOL Sept-2025) — direct evidence that a top customer can *successfully resist* Arm tightening terms, capping the take-rate ceiling. A second Qualcomm trial (antitrust/TLA) lands Q4 CY2026. [DOSSIER §8 item 4]
- **Regulators are now an explicit reversion force.** US FTC formal probe (2026-05-15), plus EU and Korea KFTC actions, examining whether Arm degrades licenses to favor its own silicon — i.e. the state is actively pushing against the very mechanism Arm would use to *raise* its take-rate. [DOSSIER §8 item 5]
- **Royalty growth is already decelerating.** Royalty growth slowed to +11% in Q4 FY2026 from +27% (Q3) — the first soft royalty print of the year. One quarter is not a trend, but it is the *direction* the model predicts. [DOSSIER §8 item 8, §7]
- **The price prices permanent disequilibrium.** Reverse-DCF requires ~35-40% FCF compounding for ~15 years (grow FCF ~30-50x), "beyond any precedent at this scale." Even if Arm hits its own aggressive FY2031 >$9 non-GAAP EPS, the 5-yr return from $408.85 is *negative* at 25-40x exit P/E. To earn 11.5%/yr the market must still pay ~76x in 2031. [DOSSIER §3.2] The price is the most disequilibrium part of the whole system.

**Disconfirming — forces that could keep Arm *away* from equilibrium for a long time (cited):**

- **A genuine network-effect moat slows reversion.** >20M developers, >325B cumulative chips, high re-port switching costs in mobile/embedded; ~99-100% smartphone-ISA share. Network effects are precisely the friction that *delays* equilibrium — they raise the activation energy a substitute must overcome. RISC-V is "NOT yet displacing Arm in smartphones or general-purpose servers." [DOSSIER §4, §4.3]
- **The royalty ladder is still climbing, not yet capped.** v9 attach target 60-70% (was ~31% mix), CSS at >10% of ASP and growing — Arm is *still* moving up the take-rate curve. Equilibrium has not yet bound; royalty revenue ($2,613M, +21%) is still compounding well above unit growth. [DOSSIER §4.2]
- **Flexible Access neutralizes part of the RISC-V cost-arbitrage.** Arm's flexible access model lowers the entry barrier and keeps cost-conscious developers inside the proprietary ecosystem, blunting the "free" advantage of RISC-V for many customers. [my research: thearmchairtrader 2026; chroniclejournal 2026] This raises the switching-cost premium and pushes the equilibrium take-rate higher than a naive "price → free substitute" model implies.
- **The sector is mid-supercycle — the whole pond is rising.** Semis +25.6% (2025) to a ~$1T run-rate, AI ~half of 2026 chip revenue; even merchant CPUs are seeing 10-15% price hikes on AI-driven shortage. A rising tide can keep Arm's *absolute* economics growing even as the *return spread* compresses — i.e. equilibrium in margins can coexist with strong revenue for a while. [DOSSIER §5; my research: KeyBanc via astutegroup/winbuzzer 2026]
- **Data center is the one place Arm is *gaining* share, not defending it.** Arm = 17.7% of total server-CPU units (up from 11.5% YoY). In a market it is *taking*, the local dynamics are disequilibrium-favorable to Arm even as the global IP-margin attractor pulls the other way. [DOSSIER §4.1]

## Reasoning

The Equilibrium lens is **highly relevant** to Arm — arguably one of the most relevant of the whole latticework here — precisely because Arm sits at the maximal-energy state of its industry: a ~98%-gross-margin toll on ~99% of a market. Physics and economics agree that such states are *defended at increasing cost*, not free. The dossier is unusually clean on the diagnostic the model cares about: **the return spread is the tell**, and Arm's clean ROIC (~13-15%) over WACC (~10.5%) is *thin* — the signature of a business **already close to its competitive equilibrium**, not one earning a durable, widening rent. A wide-moat compounder of the kind the price implies would show 30%+ ROIC and a *widening* spread. Arm shows ~12% ROE *and falling*. That single fact reframes the entire thesis: the market is paying a wide-moat-disequilibrium multiple (~231x non-GAAP) for thin-spread, near-equilibrium economics.

The lens cuts cleanest on the **AGI-CPU pivot**, and here it is close to deterministic. There are two distinct equilibria in this industry: the IP-licensing attractor (~95-98% gross, ~40-45% operating) and the merchant-silicon attractor (~40-50% gross, ~25-30% operating — *observable today* in Intel and AMD's data-center P&Ls). Arm is voluntarily migrating revenue from the first toward the second. **No amount of execution skill repeals this** — a 40%-margin dollar of silicon revenue mathematically dilutes a 98%-margin book; the only question is the speed and weight of the mix shift. The dossier already shows the leading edge of this reversion: capex 1-3%→11% of revenue, R&D 56% of revenue and rising, operating margin compressing despite +23% growth, and management/Morgan Stanley both signaling ~8 quarters of margin pressure. The model's prediction (mix shift drags blended margins toward the lower attractor) is **already being confirmed in the financials**, before the silicon business has even booked material revenue. When ~$1B (then >$2B) of 40-50%-margin AGI-CPU revenue arrives, the drag intensifies. The strategically uncomfortable point: Arm is leaving its anomalous-margin sanctuary *on purpose*, and the act of doing so simultaneously (a) lowers its own equilibrium margin and (b) detonates the channel-conflict/antitrust forces that accelerate reversion on the *legacy* royalty book too.

On the **royalty ladder**, the model is more nuanced and I hold it honestly. The ladder is the disequilibrium engine that has let royalty revenue compound ~21% on roughly flat units — genuinely impressive, and *not yet* capped (v9 attach still climbing, CSS still rising). Equilibrium has not bound here yet. But the model insists every rung raises the defection prize, and the dossier supplies the confirming forces: Arm *lost* the attempt to tighten terms on Qualcomm (the take-rate ceiling is real and was just tested in court), a free substitute is gaining at the low end and is backed by Arm's biggest customers, and regulators are now actively constraining the very mechanism Arm uses to climb. The ladder is a finite staircase, and the dossier shows Arm approaching the top of it under fire. I weight the disconfirmers — network effects and Flexible Access genuinely *slow* this reversion in mobile and raise the switching-cost premium — but slowing reversion is not escaping it; it changes the *time constant*, not the *attractor*.

**As a check on our own analysis** (the lens turned inward): the model warns *us* against assuming today's 98% margin and 17.7%-and-rising server share are a stable plateau just because they are the current reading. The momentum melt-up (price ~2.7x its 200-DMA, doubled in two weeks on a *partner's* product launch, IV percentile 100, beta 3.78) is itself a textbook disequilibrium that will mean-revert — the market is extrapolating a peak as if it were a level. Equally, the lens warns against the *opposite* over-confidence: I should not assume reversion is *fast*. Arm's moat is real and network effects have kept mobile at ~99% for a decade; the resting state could be approached slowly, and the *absolute* business can keep growing through a rising sector even as the *spread* compresses. The honest read is therefore about **distance and direction, not timing**: Arm sits far *above* its long-run equilibrium on margin, return-spread, and (most of all) price, and every observable force — substitute, buyer, regulator, and Arm's own mix-shifting strategy — points toward reversion, while only network-effect friction points toward persistence. The direction is high-confidence; the *speed* is not.

The model's verdict is therefore **negative**, and strongly so on the two things that matter most for an *investment* (as opposed to a business): the return spread is already thin and the price embeds a permanent *widening* of a spread that is in fact *narrowing*. This is not a fatal flaw in the inversion/accounting sense — Equilibrium is not a fatal-capable model, and reversion-to-a-still-decent-business is not permanent capital impairment of the enterprise. But the *gap between the equilibrium economics and the disequilibrium price* is the engine of permanent loss for an *owner at this entry point*.

## What would flip this read (disconfirmers)

- **Royalty take-rate keeps expanding for several more years with no margin give-back** — e.g. v9 attach reaches 60-70% *and* CSS pushes blended royalty/ASP materially higher *while* RISC-V share in Arm's core markets stays flat. That would say the ladder's ceiling is much higher than the model assumes and equilibrium is still distant.
- **AGI-CPU proves a *high*-margin business (>70% gross), not merchant-typical** — e.g. via CSS-style IP bundling or a fab-light model that keeps gross margins near the IP attractor. That would break prediction #3 (the mix shift would not drag the blend toward 40-50%).
- **ROIC *rises* and the spread over WACC *widens* over the next 2-3 years** (toward 20%+ clean), demonstrating Arm is moving *away* from equilibrium, not toward it. This is the cleanest single disconfirmer.
- **RISC-V stalls and a top customer *returns* to Arm on tougher terms** (e.g. Qualcomm settlement on Arm-favorable terms; antitrust probes closed with no remedy), showing the take-rate ceiling is not binding and buyer counter-pressure is weaker than the model assumes.
- **The multiple holds ~150x+ for years while earnings grow into it** — i.e. the price-level disequilibrium turns out to be a *stable* plateau because the market reprices "AI-infrastructure tax" as a permanently higher-multiple category. (The model says this is itself a disequilibrium, but a long-lived one would disconfirm the *timing* of the reversion read.)

**Bottom line:** Arm sits at the maximal-energy state of its industry — a ~98%-margin toll — but the diagnostic the model trusts most, the return spread, is already thin (ROIC ~13-15% vs ~10.5% WACC, ROE 12% and falling): the signature of a business *near* its competitive equilibrium, not one earning a durable, widening rent. Every observable force (a free RISC-V substitute, proven buyer resistance after losing Qualcomm, active FTC/EU/Korea regulators, decelerating royalties, and Arm's own voluntary mix-shift into ~40-50%-margin merchant silicon) pushes margins and returns *down* toward equilibrium, while only network-effect friction slows it. The price, meanwhile, embeds the exact opposite — a permanent, *widening* disequilibrium requiring ~35-40% FCF growth for 15 years — making the gap between near-equilibrium economics and a disequilibrium multiple the dominant risk; direction is high-confidence, speed is not.
