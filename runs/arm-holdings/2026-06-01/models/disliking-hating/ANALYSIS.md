# Disliking / Hating Tendency — Arm Holdings (ARM)

**Discipline:** Psychology  ·  **Intensity:** lens  ·  **As-of:** 2026-06-01

## What this model predicts

Munger's Disliking/Hating Tendency: humans (and the institutions they staff) who come
to dislike or hate a counterparty will **distort their conduct against it** — they
ignore or twist facts to that party's disadvantage, they refuse to cooperate, and they
will spend real money and effort to harm it **even past the point of their own narrow
self-interest**. Hatred is *self-amplifying* (it recruits confirmation bias and
in-group loyalty) and it *outlives* the original grievance. The investment translation:
a company that is widely resented by the parties who hold power over its future —
**customers** (who can defect), **regulators** (who can constrain or break it up),
**ex-employees/talent** (who can flee or seed competitors), and **nations/communities**
(who can sponsor substitutes) — carries a hidden liability that does not appear on the
balance sheet and that rational-actor models underweight.

If this model applies *unfavorably* to Arm, we should observe:
1. **A motivated, well-resourced customer adversary** acting against Arm beyond cold
   commercial logic — litigating, publicly attacking, and building escape routes.
2. **Regulators engaged with animus or hostile framing**, not merely routine review —
   probes that read Arm's intent uncharitably.
3. **Talent flight / cultural resentment** — best people leaving, ex-employees seeding
   or joining rivals, hostile internal sentiment.
4. **A community/national movement organized explicitly to displace Arm** — a "we hate
   depending on Arm" coalition funding an alternative, regardless of short-run cost.

If the model applies *favorably* (Arm is *loved*, or at least not hated, by those who
matter), we should see loyal customers, friendly regulators, talent inflow, and no
organized substitution movement. The test is which picture the evidence draws — and,
per the contract, I also turn the lens on *our own* analysis: is the bear case being
inflated by *our* disliking of an obviously overpriced, governance-compromised stock?

## Evidence

**Supporting (hatred is real, resourced, and translating into action):**

- **Qualcomm — a textbook hating-tendency adversary.** Arm sued Qualcomm/Nuvia (2022);
  the Dec-2024 jury found **no breach**, and on **Sept 30/Oct 1 2025 the court granted
  Qualcomm judgment as a matter of law** — Qualcomm headlined it a **"Complete Victory
  Over Arm."** Qualcomm then *expanded* into offense: a **second suit** (filed Apr-2024,
  amended with **antitrust + TLA** claims, going to **trial Q4 CY2026**) alleging Arm has
  a **"pattern of conduct seeking to hinder innovation"** and tried to **"obstruct
  Qualcomm's development of custom cores."** This is not a one-off contract spat — it is a
  multi-front, multi-year campaign by a **9%-of-revenue customer** to harm Arm legally,
  regulatorily, and reputationally. [DOSSIER §8; Qualcomm PR 2025-09-30; EE Times; The
  Register 2025-10-01; Futurum SITREP]
- **Qualcomm is spending to *never need Arm again*.** It acquired RISC-V designer
  **Ventana Micro (Dec-2025)** explicitly as an **Arm hedge** — the behavior the model
  predicts: a hater invests to exit the relationship even at cost. [DOSSIER §4.3; Sammy
  Fans 2025-04-03; FinancialContent 2025-12-26]
- **Regulators are engaging with hostile framing, in three jurisdictions.** Qualcomm
  seeded complaints at the **US FTC + EU Commission + Korea KFTC** (Mar-2025). KFTC ran a
  **Seoul on-site inspection (Nov-2025)**; the EU **opened an investigation (early 2026)**;
  and the **US FTC opened a FORMAL probe on 2026-05-15** examining whether Arm
  **"refuses/degrades CPU licenses to Apple/Qualcomm/Nvidia while expanding its own
  AGI-CPU business."** The framing presumes bad intent — exactly how hating-tendency
  recruits institutions. [DOSSIER §8; Tom's Hardware 2026-05; competition lane]
- **Self-inflicted channel conflict is *manufacturing* new haters out of allies.** By
  launching the **Arm AGI CPU (Mar-2026)**, Arm became a **direct competitor to its own
  royalty-paying customers** (Nvidia, AWS, Google, Microsoft, Qualcomm, Broadcom,
  Marvell). Trade press already reports customers "viewing Arm's chip business as
  competitive… potentially straining relationships built over decades." A licensor that
  competes with its licensees converts neutral/dependent customers into resentful ones —
  the precondition for defection. [DOSSIER §1, §4.3; Investing.com SWOT; tech-insider.org]
- **Talent flight + cultural resentment is visible in employee sentiment.** Glassdoor
  reviews report **"all the best people left immediately when they could, the talent is
  long gone,"** **"donated all their best talent to direct competitors,"** and questions
  about whether **"the cultural damage can ever be undone"** after recurring layoffs.
  (Counter-signal below.) Ex-Arm engineers staffing Nuvia/Qualcomm, Ampere, and RISC-V
  startups is the talent-flight mechanism in action. [Glassdoor Arm reviews, 2025–2026]
- **An organized national/ideological movement exists whose stated purpose is to escape
  Arm.** **RISC-V** is framed in 2026 trade/analyst coverage as **"China's ultimate
  weapon for semiconductor sovereignty,"** the **"third pillar"** explicitly built to
  **"bypass the Western-controlled duopoly of x86 and ARM,"** with Arm's **"proprietary
  nature and UK/US nexus… a liability in the eyes of Chinese firms."** This is
  hating-tendency at the *state* level: a buyer cohort (~19% of Arm revenue is PRC)
  motivated to displace Arm **regardless of near-term cost**, backed by industrial policy
  (Zhejiang's RISC-V targets, Jan-2026). [DOSSIER §4.3, §5; FinancialContent "Great
  Decoupling" 2025-12-30; CSIS RISC-V analyses; programming-helper 2026]
- **Even Arm's *home* sovereign was alienated.** Three UK PMs lobbied for a London
  listing; Arm chose Nasdaq, with a **primary stated reason being aversion to FCA
  related-party-transaction disclosure rules** governing its SoftBank dealings. A
  government insider's verdict: regulators "were asked to think big but they thought
  small." The UK lost its "national champion" and the episode left residual political
  friction. [The Register 2023-03; CityAM; Portfolio Adviser] *(Older, but it
  establishes the pattern: Arm structures itself to* avoid *scrutiny of exactly the
  related-party conduct now flagged as its biggest earnings-quality red flag — DOSSIER §8
  item 2.)*

**Disconfirming (the hatred is bounded; love/dependence still dominates where it counts):**

- **The people who "hate" Arm still overwhelmingly *pay* Arm.** ~**99–100% of smartphone
  app processors** and **~94% of automakers** remain on the Arm ISA; Qualcomm — the lead
  hater — **lost** its bid to break free of the license and **still ships Arm-based
  chips**. Hatred has not (yet) produced exit at the core; switching costs (>20M
  developers, software re-port cost) keep resentful customers locked in. The model's
  prediction of *defection* is, so far, **unrealized in mobile and general-purpose
  servers**. [DOSSIER §4.1, §8]
- **RISC-V's "25% penetration" is denominator-soft and concentrated in MCU/IoT** — it is
  **"winning in MCU/IoT and increasingly auto-MCUs; NOT yet displacing Arm in smartphones
  or general-purpose servers."** The dossier explicitly flags the "~25%" as tracing to
  **advocacy/AI-generated content with an ambiguous denominator.** The hating coalition is
  real but its near-term bite is overstated by the same motivated sources voicing the
  hatred. [DOSSIER §4.3, §9 item 9]
- **Arm is, by most accounts, an *admired* employer and operator.** Glassdoor overall
  **4.5/5, 89% recommend, CEO approval 93%**; recent Austin reviews are **positive**
  ("great people, optimistic outlook, good comp"). The talent-flight signal is real at the
  margin but is **not** a company-wide collapse; net hiring for the AGI-CPU build
  continues. Arm is hiring talent, not hemorrhaging it wholesale. [DOSSIER §6; Glassdoor
  Arm Austin reviews Dec-2025]
- **Regulatory animus is partly *manufactured by a litigant*, not organic public hatred.**
  The FTC/EU/KFTC actions trace substantially to **Qualcomm's complaints** — a competitor
  weaponizing antitrust, which regulators may or may not sustain. There is no consumer
  boycott, no community protest movement, no ESG/reputational pile-on of the kind that
  sinks consumer brands. Arm is an **infrastructure-layer B2B IP company** largely
  invisible to the public, which structurally caps "boycott" risk. [DOSSIER §8; judgment]
- **Some of Arm's most important counterparties are *partners and promoters*, not haters.**
  **Nvidia's** launch of Arm-based RTX Spark / Grace N1X **doubled Arm's stock** in two
  weeks; **Meta** is the AGI-CPU lead customer; **OpenAI, Cloudflare, Cerebras, SAP, SK
  Telecom** are named partners; **SoftBank** (86.4% owner) is maximally aligned. Where it
  matters most to the current narrative, Arm is being *boosted* by its ecosystem, not
  hated. [DOSSIER §1, §3, §7]

## Reasoning

**Where the model is highly relevant.** Disliking/Hating Tendency is *unusually*
applicable to Arm right now because Arm is **mid-transformation from a neutral toll-booth
into a competitor of its own customers** — the single most reliable way a B2B platform
manufactures haters. The model cleanly explains four otherwise-separate facts as one
phenomenon: (a) Qualcomm's multi-front, beyond-rational legal/regulatory campaign;
(b) the tri-jurisdiction antitrust pile-on; (c) the RISC-V "sovereignty" movement; and
(d) ex-employee talent seeding rivals. Munger's insight — that haters spend past their
own self-interest and that hatred outlives its trigger — is precisely why these threats
should be weighted *more* heavily than a rational-actor model would: Qualcomm fighting a
9%-revenue *supplier* to a courtroom "Complete Victory," and China funding a substitute
"regardless of cost," are both **animus-driven**, not purely economic, and animus is
sticky. The channel-conflict pivot is, in hating-tendency terms, **the company lighting
its own fuse**: every licensee it now competes with has a fresh, durable reason to
diversify away and to cheer on the regulators.

**Where it is a stretch / the bounding facts.** Critically, hatred has **not converted to
exit where the cash is**. The model predicts defection; the evidence shows resentful
customers *still paying* because switching costs bind. The most acute "hatred" (Qualcomm,
China-RISC-V) is also the most **self-interested and self-promoting** — which means some
of the loudest hate signal is amplified by parties (and AI-generated trade content) with
a motive to talk their own book, the mirror image of the bias the model warns about. And
Arm simultaneously enjoys genuine *affection/dependence* from Nvidia, Meta, OpenAI,
SoftBank and a 4.5/5 workforce. So the honest read is **mixed-leaning-negative**: the
hating tendency is real, multi-vector, well-funded, and *structurally increasing* because
of the silicon pivot — but it is currently **constrained by lock-in and offset by a
loud pro-Arm ecosystem**, so it is a *rising chronic liability and tail-risk amplifier*,
not an acute thesis-killer on its own.

**Turning the lens on ourselves (required for a psychology model).** Disliking/Hating
Tendency also warns *us*. It is easy, looking at a stock at **~231x non-GAAP / ~481x
GAAP**, trading **above the average sell-side target**, with **30.5% related-party
revenue**, **86.4% SoftBank control + a 72%-pledged margin loan**, **persistent dilution
and zero owner-FCF**, and **insiders only selling**, to *dislike* this company and let
that animus inflate every adverse data point. I have actively discounted for that: the
mobile/auto franchise is genuinely dominant and *loved-by-lock-in*; the RISC-V "25%" and
the AGI-CPU "$20B demand" are motivated overstatements I down-weighted; and the
regulatory pile-on is partly a competitor's lawfare, not proven misconduct. The
hating-tendency finding here is a **real but bounded negative**, and I am flagging that
the broader bear case (which lives in the valuation and incentives models) should not
double-count this lens as if it were independently fatal.

**On the fatal-flaw question.** This model is **not** fatal-capable per the contract
(the fatal-capable lenses are incentives, inversion, accounting, redundancy/margin-of-
safety). And even on its own terms, no single hatred vector is presently existential:
Qualcomm lost the first case and still pays; RISC-V hasn't cracked smartphones/servers;
talent attrition is partial; there is no consumer boycott. The credible *left tail* the
dossier names — antitrust-forced structural separation **or** a China/RISC-V substitution
cascade against the largest customer — is exactly *where this model's hatred translates
into permanent damage*, but those are tail scenarios, not the base case. So: **fatalFlaw
= false**, with the caveat that this lens meaningfully *fattens* the left tail that other
models own.

## What would flip this read (disconfirmers)

- **Customer reconciliation / renewed long-term licenses with the very parties now
  fighting** — e.g., a Qualcomm settlement-and-relicense, or Nvidia/AWS/Google publicly
  re-committing to Arm despite the AGI CPU — would show hatred is *not* translating to
  defection and flip the signal toward neutral/positive.
- **FTC/EU/KFTC closing without action** (or a narrow consent) would defang the "regulators
  hate Arm" prong and show the animus was a litigant's lawfare, not durable institutional
  hostility.
- **Arm credibly re-establishing neutrality** — e.g., spinning out or ring-fencing the
  silicon business, or a binding non-compete commitment to licensees — would *un-make*
  the haters the pivot created.
- **RISC-V failing to cross from MCU/IoT into smartphones/general-purpose servers by, say,
  FY2028**, with PRC customers still paying Arm royalties, would confirm the sovereignty
  movement's hatred lacks near-term bite.
- **Talent metrics improving** (rising Glassdoor, falling regretted attrition, marquee
  hires *into* Arm from Nvidia/Apple) would retire the talent-flight flag.
- **Conversely, a hard confirmer of the negative:** any *named* tier-1 customer publicly
  announcing a migration off the Arm ISA, or a regulator ordering structural separation,
  would escalate this from mixed-negative toward a thesis-relevant (still non-fatal-by-
  this-lens) threat.

**Bottom line:** Arm has a real and *growing* roster of motivated haters — Qualcomm (a
9%-revenue customer waging a multi-front legal/antitrust/RISC-V campaign past cold
self-interest), regulators in three jurisdictions framing Arm as a license-degrader, a
China-led "silicon sovereignty" movement built to displace it regardless of cost, and a
talent base partly soured by layoffs — and the **AGI-CPU pivot is actively manufacturing
new haters** by turning licensees into competitors. The Disliking/Hating Tendency cleanly
unifies these as one rising, sticky, off-balance-sheet liability and a left-tail amplifier
(antitrust-forced separation or a China/RISC-V cascade). But it is currently **bounded**:
hatred has not converted to exit where the royalties actually are (~99% mobile, ~94% auto
still pay Arm; Qualcomm lost and still ships), the loudest hate signal is partly motivated
overstatement, and Arm is simultaneously *boosted* by Nvidia/Meta/OpenAI/SoftBank and a
4.5/5 workforce — so the read is mixed-leaning-negative, not fatal. I also flag the
reflexive risk that *our own* dislike of this expensive, control-compromised stock could
over-weight the bear case, and have discounted for it.
