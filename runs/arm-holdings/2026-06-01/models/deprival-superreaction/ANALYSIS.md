# Deprival-Superreaction (Loss Aversion) — Arm Holdings (ARM)

**Discipline:** Psychology  ·  **Intensity:** lens  ·  **As-of:** 2026-06-01

## What this model predicts

Munger's *deprival-superreaction tendency* is his name for loss aversion plus the
endowment effect: humans overweight what they have or are about to lose, react far
more violently to taking something *away* than to never having had it, and cling to
positions — and to the stories that justify them — long past the point where reason
says let go. Kahneman/Tversky size the asymmetry at roughly 2:1 (a loss hurts about
twice as much as an equivalent gain pleases). The tendency distorts *prices* in two
opposite, exploitable ways:

1. **Capitulation (opportunity).** When a falling price forces holders through the
   pain barrier, loss aversion can flip into panic dumping — margin calls, forced
   sellers, tax-loss selling, "just make it stop" — and price *undershoots* value.
   A patient buyer is paid to absorb other people's pain. **Prediction if this is
   the live distortion in ARM: a deteriorated, fear-soaked tape — price far below a
   recent anchor, heavy/rising short interest, forced sellers, sell-side targets
   *above* price, options skew screaming for downside protection.**

2. **Refusal to sell a deteriorating story (trap).** Holders sitting on gains (or
   sitting on a beloved narrative) refuse to realize/exit because selling would
   "lock in" a loss relative to a high-water mark, or would mean admitting the story
   broke. They hold the bag; they also bid it up via the mirror-image fear — the
   **fear of missing out is loss aversion pointed at the gain you don't yet own.**
   **Prediction if this is the live distortion in ARM: a euphoric, narrative-driven
   melt-up — price *above* sell-side targets, dip-buyers refusing to let go,
   insiders quietly selling into strength, a controlling owner who will not part
   with the asset at any multiple.**

The model's job is to read *which* distortion is operating, and — per the contract —
to turn the same lens on **our own** analysis: are *we* anchoring to ARM's 52-week
high, its IPO romance, or the AI narrative, and would *we* be loss-averse about
"missing" a position?

## Evidence

**Supporting — the operative distortion is #2 (refusal/FOMO), not #1 (capitulation):**

- **Price is *above* the average analyst target — the textbook FOMO tell.** Close
  **$408.85** vs average sell-side target **~$227–241** (≈ −41% to −44% implied
  downside); even Street-high targets ($425 Mizuho, $410 Wells Fargo) sit *at/below*
  the tape, and targets are *following* price up, not leading it. [DOSSIER §3, §7]
- **A parabolic, narrative-driven melt-up.** ~doubled in two weeks to a 52-wk high;
  price ~**2.7x its 200-day MA ($152.18)**; 52-wk range $100.02–$421.69. The trigger
  was a **partner's** event (Nvidia's Arm-based RTX Spark / Grace N1X at Computex),
  not ARM's own guidance. [DOSSIER §3, §7]
- **Dip-buyers refuse to let go.** Trade-press on the 6/1 tape: "Pullbacks have been
  shallow and brief… dip buyers keep stepping in… a textbook parabolic move."
  [timothysykes.com 2026-06-01] This is the *absence* of capitulation — the opposite
  pole of the model.
- **Event asymmetry consistent with loss-averse holding of the story.** ARM's own
  record Q4 beat *fell −7.25%* (capacity miss + soft smartphone guide), yet the stock
  then doubled on someone else's launch — sentiment is anchored to the AI-ecosystem
  narrative, not to ARM's cash flows; bad own-news is shrugged off, narrative-good
  partner-news is chased. [DOSSIER §7]
- **The controlling owner is the purest endowment-effect specimen.** SoftBank (~86.4%)
  **will not sell its "crown jewel"** even at ~481x GAAP / ~231x non-GAAP earnings;
  instead it **borrows against it** — an **$8.5B margin loan** secured by **72% of the
  company pledged**, and is "tapping… undrawn margin loans secured against its stake…
  rather than divesting." [DOSSIER §6, §6.1; KeyMyHome/Bitget 2026; CNBC 2026-06-01]
  Refusing to monetize a wildly-appreciated, arguably overpriced asset — and levering
  it instead — is loss aversion / endowment behavior textbook-perfect: the asset is
  fused to identity ("vertically integrated AI powerhouse"), so selling is unthinkable
  regardless of price. (This is a *judgment* about Son's psychology, but it is
  consistent with the disclosed actions.)
- **Options market shows euphoria, not fear.** IV 108.4% with **IV Rank & Percentile
  = 100** and **beta 3.78** — extreme expected volatility at the *top* of its range,
  the signature of a crowded momentum name, not a capitulating one. [DOSSIER §7]

**Disconfirming — what argues *against* a clean loss-aversion read (and against
mistaking ARM for a capitulation bargain):**

- **The informed insiders are doing the *un*-loss-averse thing: selling into strength.**
  Insiders are **net sellers with no open-market buying** — CFO Child selling on a
  10b5-1 ramp ($148→$180→$226), Chief Architect Grisenthwaite holding **zero** direct
  shares and selling ~$5.09M, CCO Abbey trimming. [DOSSIER §6] The people with the
  most information are *not* clinging; they are realizing. That cuts against any
  notion that "smart money" is loss-averse about *not* owning ARM here, and it
  positively warns the FOMO crowd.
- **Short interest is modest — no squeeze, no forced-short capitulation to harvest.**
  ~**1.78% of float**, ~1.7 days-to-cover, easily covered. [DOSSIER §7] (A separate
  "13% of shares" figure circulates on screener sites using a different denominator;
  the float-based number is the relevant one and it is small.) There is no panicked
  short side and no forced-seller dynamic on the *long* side either.
- **No distress, no margin-call event *at ARM*.** Fortress net cash ~$3.1–3.2B, no
  bond wall. The only forced-seller mechanism is one level up (SoftBank's pledge), and
  it is dormant while the price rises. [DOSSIER §2.3, §6.1] So there is no capitulation
  to exploit today.

forced selling / short squeeze" returned **no** distress event; searches for retail

## Reasoning

**Which way is loss aversion bending ARM's price?** Decisively toward **trap, not
opportunity.** The capitulation checklist (price below anchor, heavy shorts, forced
sellers, targets above price, downside-skewed options) is *inverted* on every line:
price is above a 52-wk-high-area and above analyst targets, shorts are trivial, the
only seller pressure is informed insiders calmly distributing, and options are priced
for euphoric upside vol. There is **no fear-driven mispricing to buy.** What there *is*
is the mirror image — FOMO, the fear of *being deprived of the gain you don't yet own*
— manifesting as a refusal-to-let-go bid that has pushed price ~2.7x its 200-DMA and
past the sell-side's own targets on a partner's news. That is loss aversion working
*against* a buyer here: it is inflating the entry price, not discounting it.

**Two distinct loss-averse subjects, same direction.** (1) *The marginal buyer* is
loss-averse about missing the AI trade and is anchoring to the narrative and the
high. (2) *The controlling owner* is loss-averse/endowment-bound about its crown
jewel and will not sell at any multiple, instead levering it — which removes the most
natural supply (a SoftBank secondary at these prices) that would otherwise cool the
move. Both psychologies push the same way: they support an elevated, "sticky-high"
price that is not anchored to ARM's own cash generation (owner-FCF roughly zero-to-
negative three years running once SBC is expensed — DOSSIER §2.2).

**The genuine future risk this model flags is the *unwind*.** Deprival-superreaction
is most dangerous on the way *down*: a parabolic, FOMO-built, high-beta (3.78) name
with insiders selling is exactly the kind of position where, if the narrative cracks
(an antitrust action, a Qualcomm Case-2 loss, an AI-capex wobble, an OpenAI/Stargate
stumble — DOSSIER §8), today's "dip-buyers who refuse to let go" become tomorrow's
loss-averse bag-holders who *then* capitulate. The SoftBank pledge (72% of the company,
undisclosed trigger) is a latent forced-seller mechanism that could convert a drawdown
into a cascade with **no offer to minority ADS holders** (DOSSIER §6.1). The model
does not predict *when*, but it identifies the structure: a crowded, narrative-anchored
long with a levered, endowment-bound majority owner sitting on top is fragile, and the
*real* loss-aversion-driven capitulation (the buyable kind) would come **later and
lower**, not now.

**Check on our own bias (the contract's demand).** The strongest pull on *our*
analysis would be to treat ARM's ~456% IPO-to-date gain and AI ubiquity as a reason
to fear "missing it" — that *is* deprival-superreaction aimed at us, and we should
name it and discount it. The disciplined posture is the inverse of the crowd's: there
is no asymmetric reward in chasing a name priced above its own bulls' targets, and the
absence of a capitulation setup means this model offers **no contrarian buy signal**.
Equally, we must not over-rotate: loss aversion is a *price-distortion* lens, not a
valuation or quality verdict — it tells us *the crowd is positioned euphorically and
the price is FOMO-inflated*, which is corroborating evidence for "expensive / fragile
entry," not an independent proof of impairment. I weight it accordingly.

**Relevance.** Moderately high. ARM is an unusually clean specimen for this lens *right
now* precisely because the tape is parabolic and the owner's behavior is textbook
endowment — the model has a lot to say. It is *not* fatal-capable (it is a sentiment/
behavioral lens, not incentives/inversion/accounting/margin-of-safety), and it does
not by itself disqualify the business; it disqualifies the *entry psychology*.

## What would flip this read (disconfirmers)

- **A real capitulation setup emerges:** price falls well below a recent anchor *and*
  sell-side targets move *above* price, short interest spikes, forced/margin sellers
  appear, options skew flips hard to downside puts. *Then* loss aversion would be
  creating opportunity, not a trap, and this signal would turn from negative toward
  positive-on-weakness.
- **Insiders start *buying* on the open market** (true conviction buying, not 10b5-1
  selling) — would falsify the "informed money is distributing" read.
- **SoftBank executes a large secondary at/near these prices** — would falsify the
  endowment/refusal-to-sell read and add supply that deflates the FOMO bid.
- **Sentiment de-couples from the AI/partner narrative and re-anchors to ARM's own
  cash flows** (e.g., the stock trades on royalty trajectory, not Nvidia headlines) —
  would weaken the "story-anchored, fragile" diagnosis.
- **The float-based short interest turns out materially higher than ~1.78%** under the
  correct denominator — would reintroduce squeeze/short-capitulation dynamics I've
  ruled out.

**Bottom line:** Loss aversion is distorting ARM's price toward the **trap** pole, not
the opportunity pole. There is **no capitulation to buy** — the tape is a parabolic,
narrative-driven melt-up trading *above* its own analysts' targets at ~2.7x its
200-DMA, with dip-buyers refusing to let go (FOMO = loss aversion aimed at the gain
they don't yet own) and a controlling owner so endowment-bound it will borrow $8.5B+
against its "crown jewel" rather than sell a share. The tell that this is a trap and
not a bargain: the best-informed insiders are calmly selling into the euphoria with
zero open-market buying. The model's forward warning is that the genuinely *buyable*
loss-aversion event — real capitulation — would come **later and lower**, and the
72%-pledged SoftBank stake is a latent accelerant for that unwind.
