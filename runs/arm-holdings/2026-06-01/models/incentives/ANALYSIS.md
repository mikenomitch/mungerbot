# Reward & Punishment Superresponse (Incentives) — Arm Holdings (ARM)

**Discipline:** Psychology  ·  **Intensity:** standard  ·  **As-of:** 2026-06-01

## What this model predicts

Munger: *"Never, ever, think about something else when you should be thinking about
the power of incentives."* and *"Show me the incentive and I will show you the
outcome."* The model says: people — and especially smart, well-paid people — bend
behavior, hard, toward whatever the reward system actually pays for, often
unconsciously and often past the point of self-deception. Mis-set incentives are
the single most common cause of corporate disaster.

Applied to Arm, if incentives are *well* set we should observe:

1. **Management paid for per-share value creation** — returns on capital, durable
   free cash flow per share, prudent capital allocation — not for share-price/
   market-cap expansion they only partly control and can goose with narrative.
2. **No structural reason to pull demand forward, channel-stuff, or book
   non-arm's-length revenue** to hit a number. Revenue growth should come from
   arm's-length customers transacting at market prices.
3. **The controlling shareholder's incentives aligned with the minority's** —
   same share class, same exit, no mechanism by which the controller can be made
   *better off* by an action that leaves the float worse off.
4. **Insiders behaving like owners** — buying when they believe, holding, low
   dilution, capital returned to shareholders when reinvestment runway thins.
5. **The sales channel and customers incentivized to deepen, not diversify away
   from, the platform.**

If incentives are *badly* set, we see the inverse: pay tied to multiple/price
rather than cash; a related-party revenue machine the controller both prices and
funds; a controller who can be forced to dump the stock or who profits at the
float's expense; insiders as net sellers; and a strategy that gives the biggest
customers a reason to leave. The model is **fatal-capable** here: an incentive
structure that rewards inflating reported numbers via a captive related party,
combined with a controller who is a leveraged forced-seller, is exactly the
configuration that produces permanent capital loss.

## Evidence

**Disconfirming the "good incentives" thesis (i.e. supporting a NEGATIVE read):**

- **CEO pay rewards market-cap milestones, not per-share cash.** The proposed
  one-off grant (~2026-06-01 AGM) of **425,000 PSUs** vests on **market-
  capitalization** hurdles — **$1T by 31-Mar-2029, $1.5T by 2030, $2T by 2031** —
  worth **~$800M–$1bn+**; the cash-bonus/award cap was raised 125%→200% of salary.
  [Dossier §6; New Electronics 2026-06; Electronics Weekly 2026-06] The milestones
  pay for **multiple/share-price expansion, not return on capital or FCF/share.**
  At a ~$437B cap today, hitting $2T is a ~4.6x re-rate; a CEO so incentivized is
  rewarded for *feeding the narrative and the multiple*, which is precisely the
  behavior the price already reflects (priced at ~231x non-GAAP / ~481x GAAP TTM,
  *above* the average sell-side target). [Dossier §3]
- **FY2026 CEO single-figure ~$59.6–60.6M, up ~143% YoY, ~4x the size-peer median
  (~$14.7M)**, only ~2.2% salary. [Dossier §6] Large, equity-weighted, price-
  sensitive pay magnifies the milestone incentive.
- **The "approval" of pay is not a market check.** Say-on-pay and the PSU grant are
  decided by **SoftBank's ~86.4% vote**; minority holders "have effectively no
  governance leverage." [Dossier §6; 20-F FY2026] So the one mechanism that
  normally disciplines self-serving pay design is absent.
- **A captive, controller-priced, controller-funded related-party revenue engine.**
  Related parties = **$1,499M = 30.5% of FY2026 revenue, +82% YoY** (vs +7%
  external) — **the majority of reported growth.** Within it: **Arm China IPLA
  $790.6M** (Arm relies on **Arm China self-reported royalty data**; Arm holds only
  ~4.8% indirect economic interest) + a **SoftBank-affiliate Consulting Agreement
  $704.4M (+384%)**, of which **~$645.8M is an UNBILLED contract asset** owed by a
  SoftBank-controlled counterparty. [Dossier §8.2; 20-F FY2026 Notes 4 & 20] My
  own check of interim filings confirms this asset *ramped through the year* —
  ~$351.5M (Sep-2025) → ~$498.7M (Dec-2025) → ~$645.8M (Mar-2026). [Arm 6-K
  2025-09-30 & 2025-12-31, SEC EDGAR] The incentive read: SoftBank **sets the
  price** on these deals (related-party process, not negotiation) **and owes the
  cash**, and the revenue lands in exactly the window when SoftBank is motivated to
  show Arm growth (margin-loan collateral value; SoftBank Group narrative).
- **The controller is a leveraged, conflicted, potential forced-seller.** SoftBank
  holds **~86.4%**, has **pledged ~72% of the company** against an **$8.5B margin
  loan**, and the proceeds reportedly help fund SoftBank's **~$22.5B OpenAI** and
  Stargate commitments. [Dossier §6.1] Trade press describes the facility expanded
  toward ~$20B across 33 banks and SoftBank concentrating ~54.6% of its NAV in Arm,
  with an estimated margin-call breach near ~$37/share at 25% LTV. [Bloomberg
  2025-10-10; MEXC/MarketScreener 2025-10] *Magnitudes are secondary/undisclosed*,
  but the **incentive direction is unambiguous**: the controller is paid to keep
  Arm's price high (collateral) and may be *forced to sell or trigger a change of
  control with no offer to the minority* if it falls — an interest that can diverge
  sharply from the float's.
- **CEO conflict of attention/loyalty.** On **2026-04-21** SoftBank named Haas
  **CEO of SoftBank Group International** (running the parent's chip/AI portfolio)
  **while he remains Arm CEO/director**, with **no disclosed safeguards.** [Dossier
  §6; Arm 6-K 2026-04-21] His incentive now formally includes optimizing for the
  *parent*, which both prices the related-party deals and is the leveraged holder.
- **Insiders are net sellers; no conviction buying.** 34 Form 144s / 26 Form 4s in
  the recent window; **CFO Child a steady 10b5-1 seller** (~$3.1M @ $148 → ~$7.23M
  @ $226, Mar–May 2026); **Chief Architect Grisenthwaite holds ZERO direct shares**
  and sold ~$5.09M; Haas holds only ~0.037% directly. **No open-market buying
  observed.** [Dossier §6] Insiders are monetizing, not accumulating.
- **Capital is returned to employees (equity), not owners.** **No dividend, no real
  buyback;** SBC ~$1,052M (~21% of revenue); token repurchases (~$119M/qtr) do not
  offset SBC (~$285M/qtr); diluted share count **rose every year** (1,025M→1,068M).
  [Dossier §2.6] Owners are diluted to pay the labor force that the milestone-pay
  structure also rewards.
- **The new strategy gives the biggest customers a reason to leave.** Selling the
  in-house **Arm AGI CPU** (Mar-2026) makes Arm a **direct competitor to its own
  royalty-paying customers** (Nvidia, AWS, Google, Microsoft, Broadcom, Marvell),
  incentivizing them toward in-house designs and **RISC-V**. [Dossier §4.3] The
  channel's incentive flips from "deepen Arm dependence" to "hedge it."

**Supporting the "good incentives" thesis (i.e. mitigating the negative read):**

- The PSU plan is at least **performance-linked and steeply backloaded** (vesting
  needs a ~2x–4.6x re-rate over 3–5 years); it is not a free RSU cliff, and it does
  align Haas with *some* shareholder outcome (price), even if the wrong metric.
  [New Electronics 2026-06]
- **No current material weakness; prior weakness remediated FY2025; no restatement;
  clawback box unchecked; Deloitte auditor; controls effective 2026-03-31.**
  [Dossier §6] The accounting *plumbing* is clean even if the related-party
  *substance* is a question; the Critical Audit Matter is revenue recognition on
  long-term contracts, i.e. the auditor is looking at the right risk.
- **Haas is a strong, well-regarded operator** (Glassdoor CEO approval 93%; doubled
  revenue since IPO). [Dossier §6] Reputation is itself an incentive that cuts
  toward not torching the franchise.
- The controller's pledge cuts **both** ways: SoftBank is *also* hugely incentivized
  to **protect** Arm's price (it is the collateral and ~54.6% of NAV), so day-to-day
  it wants Arm to do well. The forced-seller risk is a **tail**, not a base case.
- External (arm's-length) revenue still **grew +7%** and the core royalty ladder
  (v8→v9→CSS) is a genuine, incentive-compatible flywheel where customers pay more
  because they get more. [Dossier §2.1, §4.2]

## Reasoning

**On the subject (management, controller, channel, customers).** This is a
high-relevance model for Arm — incentives are not a footnote here, they are close
to the whole governance story. The structure is, in Munger's terms, a textbook
**"man-with-a-hammer"-meets-conflict-of-interest** setup, and the hammer is
*reported growth and market cap*:

- The CEO's headline new award pays out on **market cap**, not per-share economics.
  Munger would predict — and the tape already shows — behavior optimized for the
  *multiple* and the *narrative* (the AGI-CPU "new business," the >$1.5T TAM, the
  $15B/$100B aspirational targets) rather than for the thin **~13–15% ROIC vs ~10%
  WACC** reality. [Dossier §2.4, §5] When you pay for market cap in a momentum
  stock, you pay for storytelling.
- The related-party machine is the part that engages the **fatal-capable** edge of
  the model. SoftBank **controls the price-setting, controls the vote that blesses
  the related-party process, controls the auditor-change consent, owes the
  $645.8M**, and **benefits from the reported growth** (collateral value, parent
  narrative, and — via the margin loan — *liquidity for OpenAI*). That is four
  incentives all pointing the same way: **toward larger, earlier, controller-
  sourced revenue recognition.** Independently, Arm *relies on Arm China's self-
  reported royalties* with only ~4.8% economic interest — a second related party
  where Arm cannot even verify the base. When the entity that prices the
  transaction, funds it, and votes the proxy is the same entity, the model's
  default prediction is that the number will be **flattered**, and the float bears
  the realization risk. This is not proof of fraud — controls are clean and there's
  no restatement — but it is precisely the incentive configuration that *precedes*
  earnings-quality blowups, and it is **30.5% of revenue and the majority of
  growth.** I treat it as a **credible (not yet realized) path** toward impaired
  reported earnings and, in a stress, permanent loss.
- The margin loan converts the controller into a **leveraged, price-dependent
  holder whose interests can flip against the minority in a drawdown.** In good
  times SoftBank protects the price (aligned). In a sharp fall — exactly when the
  minority most needs a steady hand — SoftBank may be *forced* to sell or accept a
  change of control **with no offer to ADS holders**, and is simultaneously bleeding
  the asset to fund OpenAI. The float is, in effect, **short a put it didn't write**
  against SoftBank's liquidity needs. A WSJ report that OpenAI missed targets
  already moved ARM ~−8% on the week [Dossier §6.1] — the transmission mechanism is
  live.
- The **channel/customer** incentive has just been **inverted by Arm's own hand.**
  For 35 years the incentive-compatible deal was "we stay neutral IP, you build on
  us." Launching merchant silicon tells Nvidia/AWS/Google/MS/Broadcom that Arm will
  compete with them — the rational response is to fund the substitute (RISC-V) and
  internalize design. This is **empire-building** (Haas: "a new business for Arm")
  that the milestone-pay structure *rewards* (it grows reported TAM and cap) while
  **eroding the moat** that produced the high-margin royalty in the first place.

**On our own analytical bias (the model turned on us).** The discipline demands I
apply incentive/psychology checks to *our* reasoning too:

- **Reciprocation/liking + authority.** Haas is genuinely impressive and likeable
  (93% approval); the deck is slick; SoftBank is a famous name. Munger warns these
  trigger over-trust. I should *discount* the comfort that "a great operator runs
  it" — great operators paid on market cap still optimize for market cap.
- **Social proof / commitment-and-consistency.** Consensus is "Buy," estimates are
  being revised up, the stock doubled. The model predicts analysts are *following
  price* (their own incentive is to not be short a momentum name) — and the dossier
  confirms targets now *lag* the tape ($227–241 avg vs $409). I must not let the
  crowd's revisions substitute for the incentive analysis.
- **Incentive-caused bias in *me*.** A "find the fatal flaw" mandate can over-
  reward drama. I have therefore required the negative read to rest on *structure*
  (who prices/funds/votes), not on any allegation of wrongdoing — and I explicitly
  credit the clean audit, remediated weakness, and arm's-length core growth.

Net: the incentive architecture is **materially misaligned with the minority
shareholder** across pay (paid for cap, not cash), revenue quality (controller-
priced/funded growth), governance (no minority check, forced-seller tail), capital
return (owners diluted), and strategy (customers given a reason to leave). The
core royalty business has *good* incentives; the *wrapper* — controller, pay,
related parties, leverage — does not.

## What would flip this read (disconfirmers)

- **Pay re-tied to per-share economics.** If the PSU plan were amended (or
  supplemented) to gate on FCF/share, ROIC-above-WACC, or non-GAAP-EPS-net-of-SBC
  rather than market cap, the central misalignment would soften materially.
- **Related-party revenue shrinks toward immateriality / is independently
  verified.** External (arm's-length) growth re-accelerating above the related-
  party bucket, the $645.8M unbilled asset **converting to cash on schedule**, and
  Arm China royalties becoming auditable (not self-reported) would defuse the
  fatal-capable thread. Several consecutive quarters of related-party share
  *declining* would do it.
- **SoftBank de-levers / unpledges.** Repayment or material reduction of the margin
  loan, or an explicit minority-protection (tag-along / mandatory offer on change
  of control), removes the forced-seller tail.
- **Insider conviction buying** — open-market purchases by Haas/Child/Grisenthwaite
  at these prices — would be a genuine incentive-aligned signal (and none exists).
- **Independent board / minority say-on-pay with teeth** (loss of controlled-
  company posture) would restore the disciplining mechanism.
- **A clean, audited demonstration** that related-party pricing matches third-party
  comparables (e.g., a fairness opinion or auditor walk-through in the CAM) would
  weaken the "flattered number" inference.

**Bottom line:** Arm's *core* royalty business has incentive-compatible economics,
but the *wrapper* around it is badly aligned with the minority shareholder: the CEO
is newly paid up to ~$800M–$1bn+ on **market-cap** milestones (storytelling, not
cash per share); a third of revenue and the majority of growth is **related-party**,
priced and partly funded by the very controller who benefits from it and votes the
proxy; and that controller has pledged 72% of the company to fund OpenAI, making it
a leveraged, price-dependent **potential forced-seller with no offer to the float.**
Insiders sell and never buy, owners get dilution instead of capital return, and the
new merchant-silicon strategy hands Arm's biggest customers a reason to defect.
Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome: this set of incentives rewards
**inflating reported scale and the multiple** — precisely what the ~231x/~481x
price already embodies — while leaving the minority exposed to the controller's
liquidity needs and to the realization risk on revenue it cannot independently
verify. Strongly negative; flagged fatal-capable on the related-party/forced-seller
combination.
