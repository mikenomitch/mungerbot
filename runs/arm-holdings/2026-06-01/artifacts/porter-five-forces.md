# Arm Holdings — Porter Five Forces (as-of 2026-06-01)

Evidence-based read by the `competition` lane. Ratings are this analyst's structured judgment over the cited facts (labeled as judgment per desk rules); facts in `../research/competition/NOTES.md`.

| Force | Intensity | Why (evidence) |
|---|---|---|
| **Rivalry among existing competitors** | **MODERATE→HIGH and rising** | Arm has near-monopoly ISA share in mobile (~99–100%) and embedded, so head-to-head ISA rivalry is historically low. But rivalry is intensifying on two fronts: (1) **x86** (Intel+AMD) in data center, where Arm is *gaining* (17.7% server units Q1'26 vs 11.5% YoY); (2) Arm's own **March 2026 entry into selling finished AGI CPUs** turns Arm into a direct rival of Broadcom, Marvell, and even its hyperscaler customers' merchant-silicon ambitions. Pricing power has been *up* (royalty ladder v8→v9→CSS), not competed away — evidence rivalry has not yet eroded economics. |
| **Threat of new entrants / substitutes (ISA-level)** | **HIGH and structurally rising** | The defining force. **RISC-V** is a free, open-source ISA explicitly named in Arm's 20-F as a competitive threat; "many of our customers are also major supporters of RISC-V." Backers include Qualcomm, Google, Meta, Nvidia, Bosch/Infineon/NXP (auto consortium), Alibaba/Ventana (servers), and China's "silicon sovereignty" drive. Substitution is real but **uneven**: advanced in MCU/IoT/embedded and gaining in auto MCUs; *not yet* displacing Arm in smartphones or general-purpose servers (no incumbent SW stack to displace; ecosystem immature). A second substitute: workloads migrating CPU→AI accelerators (TPU/Trainium/MTIA/GPU) could shrink the CPU TAM Arm taxes (20-F-flagged). |
| **Bargaining power of buyers (customers)** | **MODERATE→HIGH** | Extreme concentration: top-5 customers ≈ 56% of revenue; largest (Arm China) ≈ 17%. Buyers are giant, sophisticated, cash-rich (Apple, Qualcomm, Nvidia, AWS, Google, Microsoft) and several can credibly threaten the *ultimate* outside option — designing their own cores (Apple, Qualcomm/Nuvia) or defecting to RISC-V. The **Qualcomm/Nuvia litigation (Qualcomm won, Dec 2024 jury; affirmed Sep–Oct 2025)** demonstrated a top customer successfully resisting Arm's attempt to tighten license terms — a visible loss of buyer-side leverage for Arm. Offsetting: switching the ISA carries huge software-ecosystem cost, which caps buyer power in the near term. |
| **Bargaining power of suppliers** | **LOW** | Arm is an IP/design house; it does not buy wafers (until the AGI CPU, where TSMC 3nm fabrication introduces *some* supplier dependence on TSMC and OSAT/packaging). Core inputs are R&D talent and EDA tools (Synopsys/Cadence) — meaningful but not a chokehold. Historically asset-light/supplier-light; the silicon pivot modestly raises supplier exposure (foundry capacity, CoWoS-class packaging). |
| **Threat of substitutes (business-model)** | **MODERATE** | Beyond RISC-V, the substitute is "vertical integration by customers" — hyperscalers and Apple internalizing design. They still (mostly) pay Arm an architecture license + royalty, so it is *partial* substitution that pressures rate/mix rather than eliminating Arm. If a major customer moved a flagship line to RISC-V end-to-end, that would convert latent substitution into realized share loss. |

## Net moat read (judgment, for downstream model agents to weigh)

- **Strongest moat sources:** (1) **Ecosystem / software-compatibility network effect** — >20M developers, "the vast majority of the world's software," and high switching costs to re-port/re-validate; this is the load-bearing moat. (2) **Standards incumbency / installed base** — >310B cumulative chips create de-facto-standard gravity in mobile/embedded. (3) **Scale economics in R&D** — one ISA amortized across the industry.
- **Weakest / eroding moat edges:** (1) **No legal/patent lock on the ISA concept** — RISC-V is a clean-room open alternative; the moat is ecosystem, not exclusivity. (2) **Buyer concentration + buyers-as-rivals** — the same firms that pay Arm fund RISC-V and design in-house. (3) **Self-inflicted channel conflict** — selling the AGI CPU and the (lost) Qualcomm suit + **FTC antitrust probe (reported May 2026)** raise the odds customers diversify away to reduce dependence on a vendor that now competes with them.
- **Durability verdict (judgment):** the moat is **wide but softening at the edges**. Mobile/embedded incumbency is durable on a 3–5yr view; the *new growth* (data center, finished silicon) is being won in a more contested arena and carries regulatory and customer-trust risk that did not exist when Arm was a pure neutral-IP "Switzerland."
