# DOSSIER — Urban Outfitters, Inc. (Nasdaq: URBN) — as-of 2026-06-02

**Editor's note.** This is the merged, de-duplicated, cited evidence base for the Munger
mental-model run. It synthesizes eight research lanes (filings, financials, valuation, news,
leadership, competition, sector, prediction-markets). The desk **collects facts and does not
opine**; verdicts are formed downstream. Where lanes disagree, the conflict is shown and
adjudicated inline — look for **[RECONCILED]**. Every material fact carries a source; judgments
are marked as judgments. Fiscal convention: **FY ends January 31 and is labeled by the year it
ends in** (FY2026 = year ended 2026-01-31; the most recent reported period is **Q1 FY2027**, the
quarter ended 2026-04-30, reported 2026-05-20). Dollar figures are US$ millions unless noted.

**One-paragraph orientation.** Urban Outfitters is a founder-controlled, **debt-free**, multi-brand
specialty-apparel house running three economic engines: **Retail** (~85.7% of sales — Anthropologie,
Free People incl. FP Movement, and the namesake Urban Outfitters), **Subscription/Nuuly** (~9.2% — a
clothing-rental business that just turned its first annual profit), and **Wholesale** (~5.1%, mostly
Free People). FY2026 was a **record year: revenue $6,165.4M (+11.1%)** with **10-year-high gross
(36.0%) and operating (9.8%) margins**, **record net income $464.9M**, ROIC comfortably above WACC,
and a **net-cash balance sheet**. The business has now printed **seven consecutive record quarters**
through Q1 FY2027. The accounting is clean (Deloitte unqualified on both financials and ICFR; no
restatement, no material weakness). The two things that dominate the investment question are:
(1) **durability** — seven straight record quarters and peak margins in a notoriously cyclical,
low-barrier fashion industry invite extrapolation, and the edge in ~91% of the business is
*perishable brand/merchandising taste*, not a structural moat (the one durable niche is Nuuly's
~64% US apparel-rental share, but that is only ~9% of sales); and (2) **valuation** — at **$72.26**
the stock is roughly fairly valued to modestly rich (blended intrinsic ~$50/$65/$95 bear/base/bull;
**margin of safety ~0%**), trading at a clear premium to apparel peers (13.9x P/E vs Abercrombie's
7.3x) on near-identical financials. Governance is a double-edged sword: strong owner-operator
alignment (CEO Richard Hayne owns 20%, $1 salary; insiders ~32%) but **deep family entrenchment**
(spouse, son, and nephew in senior roles), a **78-year-old founder-Chairman-CEO with no disclosed
succession plan**, and incentives keyed to **size/operating-income, not per-share value or return on
capital**. The crowd is split: sell-side Moderate Buy (~$87 target) vs. **rising ~12%-of-float short
interest**, and a +16% Q1 EPS beat that moved the stock only ~+3% — i.e., the bull case is largely
priced.

## 1. Business & Segments

- **What URBN is.** A Pennsylvania-incorporated, single-class (one-share-one-vote) specialty-retail
  holding company listed on Nasdaq Global Select (ticker **URBN**, CIK 0000912615). It operates a
  portfolio of lifestyle brands — **not** a single banner — across three reportable segments. [RUN.md;
  FY2026 10-K Item 1 & Note 17]
- **Three reportable segments (FY2026 net sales, with segment operating margin):**
  - **Retail ~85.7% — $5,282.7M (+7.9%)**, segment op margin **10.9%**. Brands: Anthropologie, Free
    People (incl. FP Movement activewear), Urban Outfitters namesake.
  - **Subscription / Nuuly ~9.2% — $568.4M (+50.2%)**, segment op margin **6.1%**. Apparel-rental.
  - **Wholesale ~5.1% — $314.3M (+14.0%)**, segment op margin **19.7%** (highest-margin segment;
    primarily Free People). [filings lane, 10-K Note 17]
- **[RECONCILED] Nuuly segment profitability.** The financials and competition lanes both flagged
  Nuuly standalone profit as a data gap (press releases give segment *net sales* only). The **filings
  lane pulled the 10-K segment-income footnote**: Nuuly swung from a **−$17.1M segment loss (FY2024)**
  to **+$34.9M segment income (FY2026)** while ~2.4x-ing sales — its **first full annual profit
  (~$35M, 6.1% segment op margin)**. This is now a confirmed primary-filing fact, not secondary
  inference. [filings lane, 10-K Note 17 — supersedes the financials/competition data gaps]
- **Brand-level revenue & comps (FY2026):**

  | Brand (net sales) | FY2026 | FY2025 | YoY % | FY26 comp |
  |---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
  | Anthropologie | 2,586.6 | 2,426.4 | +6.6% | +5.9% |
  | Free People (incl. wholesale) | 1,618.4 | 1,460.3 | +10.8% | +4.8% |
  | Urban Outfitters (namesake) | 1,351.6 | 1,247.7 | +8.3% | +7.3% |
  | Nuuly | 568.4 | 378.4 | +50.2% | n/a |
  | Menus & Venues (food/bev) | 40.3 | 37.8 | +6.6% | — |

  Within Retail, the FY2026 mix is roughly Anthropologie ~49%, Free People ~25%, Urban Outfitters
  ~25%. [financials lane; competition lane]
- **THE KEY OPERATING SIGNAL — a brand-comp inversion.** The long-troubled **namesake Urban
  Outfitters brand turned**, swinging from −3% (Q3 FY26) to **+9.6% / +9.3%** (Q4 FY26 / Q1 FY27),
  while **Anthropologie — the largest, most profitable retail brand — decelerated to +3.7% then
  +1.9%** (Q1 FY27). The namesake turnaround is real but only ~2 quarters old; the Anthropologie
  slowdown is the offsetting watch item (see §4 — consistent with Aritzia taking share). Management is
  shrinking UO store footprints (~10k → 6–7k sq ft) and closing underperformers. [news lane; competition lane]
- **Store fleet (793 stores at 2026-01-31):** 254 Anthropologie, 180 Free People + 88 FP Movement,
  253 Urban Outfitters, 9 Menus & Venues. **FP Movement is the unit-growth engine** (+25 stores,
  +44.6% selling sq ft); the **namesake is in net contraction** (−12 stores, −2.0% sq ft). [filings; competition]
- **Nuuly mechanics (the structural growth/bull story).** FY2026 net sales $568.4M, ~420k average
  active subscribers (+~40–45% YoY), now ~10% of revenue; URBN guides toward **$1B+**. Capital- and
  inventory-intensive (rental inventory ~$230.1M, +19%). In March 2026 URBN **bought the previously-
  leased Raymore, MO fulfillment center** (part of the elevated FY2027 capex). [news lane; filings lane]
- **Workforce & sourcing.** ~31,000 employees (~38% full-time), non-union. ~4,000 vendors, **none >10%
  of purchases**, dual-sourced, **no single country a majority of production** — a deliberate tariff/
  supply-chain hedge. [filings lane, 10-K Item 1]

## 4. Moat & Competition

**Detail in [`artifacts/competitor-matrix.md`](../artifacts/competitor-matrix.md) and
[`artifacts/porter-five-forces.md`](../artifacts/porter-five-forces.md).**

- **Industry structure: low-barrier, hyper-competitive.** URBN's own 10-K states the specialty-retail
  and wholesale apparel industries "are each highly competitive," competing on store location, digital
  UX, merchandise breadth/quality/style/price, service, and (newly emphasized) **AI capability**. The
  10-K **names no specific competitors**; Anthropologie/Free People "face competition from small
  boutiques." Fragmented, no hard structural moat in the core. [competition lane, 10-K Item 1 & 1A]
- **The core edge is PERISHABLE brand + merchandising taste — not a structural moat.** Munger-lens
  read across the disciplines: **no switching costs** (no contracts; Nuuly is 1-click-cancel),
  **modest scale economies** (shared sourcing/back-office on $6.17B — but **Aritzia thrives at
  ~$3.7B**, so scale is not decisive), **essentially no network effects**, **no regulatory/IP barriers**
  (trademarks only). Evidenced by the namesake UO brand's *own* prior multi-year slump and
  Abercrombie-brand's −10% comp — fashion brands cool. **Only Nuuly looks structurally moated, and it
  is ~9% of sales.** [competition lane — judgment, well-evidenced]
- **THE ONE DURABLE ADVANTAGE — Nuuly (apparel rental):** holds ~**64% of the US apparel-rental
  market** (secondary: Wells Fargo via Retail Dive), **>2.5x Rent the Runway** on subscribers (~420k
  vs RTR ~144k). Wins via (1) **sourcing ~45% of rental inventory AT COST from sister brands** — a cost
  edge no standalone renter can match; (2) **~70% of subscribers are new to rental** (category
  expansion, not poaching); (3) **profitable while rivals burn cash**; (4) a 600k-sqft fulfillment
  center sized for ~3x current subs. [competition lane]
- **Nuuly's #2 rival is distressed:** **Rent the Runway (RENT)** — ~143,796 active subscribers
  (1/31/26), **negative FCF −$46M**, an **equity deficit**, a debt recapitalization whose **$96.3M
  restructuring gain** drove its only GAAP "net income," and **founder-CEO Jennifer Hyman stepped
  down**. VC entrants (Vivrelle, Pickle) raised tens of millions in 2024–25 but are sub-scale. The
  **capital barrier in rental is real** — RTR's burn proves it (and is the moat protecting Nuuly).
  [competition lane]
- **THE SHARPEST THREAT TO THE CORE — Aritzia (ATZ.TO):** FY2026 net revenue $3.7B (+35.2%), **US
  revenue +43.8% to $2.28B**, comp **+19%**, opening 8–10 US stores/yr toward 150+ (~$10M revenue per
  new store on a $4M build; 12–18mo payback). It attacks the **exact "elevated women's / everyday
  luxury" positioning of Anthropologie and Free People**, growing ~3x faster than URBN's
  mid-single-digit Retail comps. **Anthropologie's deceleration to +1.9% comp is consistent with share
  loss** (inference — URBN discloses no share figures). [competition lane — judgment]
- **Porter Five Forces (net):** Rivalry **HIGH** (Retail/Wholesale), moderate (Nuuly today); new
  entrants **MODERATE** (low barriers to a DTC brand, high capital barrier in rental); buyer power
  **HIGH** (zero switching costs, discretionary, promotional, concentrated wholesale buyers); supplier
  power **LOW–MODERATE/favorable** (fragmented vendors; Nuuly sources internally; tariffs are the real
  input pressure); substitutes **MODERATE–HIGH** (off-price, resale, fast fashion, "buy-less,"
  rental-vs-ownership). [competition lane]
- **A genuine tailwind — fast fashion losing US momentum:** **SHEIN US apparel sales −4.5% in 2025**,
  US share 1.8%→1.7% (first decline since 2021) after the **de-minimis exemption ended (2025-08-29)**
  and tariffs raised landed costs — easing price pressure on full-price specialty retailers,
  especially the value-led UO namesake. [competition lane; sector lane]
- **Competitor map by arena:** elevated women's (Anthro/Free People) vs **Aritzia [primary]**,
  Reformation, Abercrombie, Madewell/J.Crew, Banana Republic; activewear (FP Movement) vs **Lululemon,

## 5. Sector & Macro

**Detail in [`artifacts/sector-data.md`](../artifacts/sector-data.md).**

- **Mature, low-growth, hyper-competitive.** US apparel market ~$365–395B (Statista $373B 2026)
  growing only **~1.8–2.4% CAGR**; McKinsey/BoF *State of Fashion 2026* puts US/China at **+1–3%**.
  Industry economics are structurally modest/volatile: net margins ~2–10%, **>40% of fashion goods
  eventually marked down**, ~25% online return rates. **The durable franchise sits in company-level
  brand/merchandising execution, not in the industry.** [sector lane]
- **TARIFFS = the dominant 2026 sector variable.** The US imports ~**89% of apparel & leather goods**.
  2025 trade actions pushed effective apparel tariffs from ~12–17% MFN to **30–49%** across key
  countries (China ~34%, Vietnam ~46%, Bangladesh ~37%, Cambodia ~49%, Indonesia ~32%, Sri Lanka ~44%);
  **Mexico/USMCA at 0%** is the nearshoring escape valve. ~75% of execs plan price hikes. The forward
  tariff path is **policy-set and genuinely uncertain** — a high-impact known unknown. [sector lane]
  - **URBN-specific tariff exposure (from MD&A):** the company imports substantially all merchandise.
    The **Feb-2026 Supreme Court invalidation of many IEEPA tariffs** led to new "incremental global
    tariffs on all imports"; the **refund process is unfinalized**; management explicitly warns tariffs
    "could have a negative impact on our financial results" even after mitigation (dual-sourcing,
    air-to-ocean freight shift, "gently raising prices"). Quantified drag: **~−60 to −75 bps gross
    margin/quarter** plus **~−70 bps/quarter Middle-East fuel-surcharge** guided through FY2027.
    [filings lane MD&A; news lane]
- **Circular / ownership-model shift — double-edged for URBN.** US secondhand grew ~13% in 2025
  (~3.6–4x broader retail), heading to **$78.8B by 2030**; 58% Gen Z / 55% Millennials prioritize
  secondhand. **Online clothing rental** (Nuuly's niche) is small (~$2–3B) but grows ~**9.6–11.3%
  CAGR**, effectively a Nuuly/Rent-the-Runway duopoly. This **reallocates wallet away from full-price
  new-apparel retail (threat to the Retail core) while validating the access-over-ownership thesis
  behind Nuuly.** [sector lane; ThredUp 14th Report Apr 2026]
- **Cycle position: late-cycle, K-shaped, decelerating.** OBBBA tax cuts (effective 2026-01-01) drove
  an early-2026 apparel-spend wave (clothing stores +9.39% YoY Jan-2026), but by Feb–Mar 2026 consumer
  confidence dipped (a 12-yr-low figure cited), discretionary unit demand fell **−3% YoY** (Circana),
  and **46% of fashion execs expect 2026 to deteriorate** (only 25% expect improvement). Value brands
  gain share even among higher-income shoppers. **The tax-cut tailwind likely masks underlying
  value-seeking and tariff cost pressure** — the macro backdrop that makes URBN's 7-quarter record
  streak vulnerable to mean reversion. [sector lane]
- **Secondary drivers:** apparel e-commerce ~43% of the category and rising (Forrester names specialty
  retailers as **most pressured** by the channel shift); modest mall-traffic counter-positive via
  experiential repositioning; a **GLP-1 wardrobe-refresh tailwind** (12.4% of Americans on weight-loss
  drugs, 2x prior — size-mix shifts + "revenge shopping," a natural fit for try-different-sizes rental);
  **AI** cited as the industry's top opportunity (a margin/inventory lever more than a revenue one).
  [sector lane]
- **The 2–3 variables that drive sector economics:** (1) discretionary consumer health / cycle
  position; (2) tariffs & sourcing cost; (3) channel & ownership-model shift. **Normalized sector
  ROIC:** no clean public series found (data gap) — directionally **modest and volatile**;
  above-cost-of-capital returns are earned by **disciplined operators with brand pricing power, not by
  the industry as a class** (the median competitor likely does not earn its cost of capital through a
  full cycle). [sector lane — judgment]
