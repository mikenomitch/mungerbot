- **U.S. imports ~89% of apparel & leather goods** → sector is among the most tariff-exposed. (Clarkston 2026; FashionUnited)
- Disclosed corporate hits: **Tapestry ~$160M (~230 bps margin)**; **Victoria's Secret ~$100M (2025)**. (Clarkston 2026)
- Mitigations in flight: **SKU rationalization** (Tapestry cut handbag styles >30%; H&M cut SKUs 15–25% 2023–25), sourcing diversification (China share −6% 2019–23; Vietnam exports +35% 2015–20), nearshoring to Mexico. (Clarkston 2026)
- **~75% of fashion executives plan price increases in 2026; ~45% of North American firms intend hikes >5%.** (McKinsey/BoF SoF 2026)
- Apparel price inflation running above general CPI; some forecasts of fashion prices up to +17% as tariffs pass through. (Retaildive / FashionUnited 2025–26)

## The 2–3 variables that most drive sector economics

1. **Discretionary consumer health / cycle position** — apparel is among the first categories cut in a downturn; the 2026 read is late-cycle, K-shaped, value-seeking (mitigated near-term by OBBBA tax cuts).
2. **Tariffs & sourcing cost** — the defining 2026 input-cost shock; ~89% import dependence, effective rates up from ~12–17% to 30–49% across key countries; determines gross margin and pricing power.
3. **Channel & ownership-model shift** — e-commerce (~43% of apparel) plus the **circular** shift (resale ~13% growth; rental ~10% CAGR) is reallocating spend away from full-price new-apparel retail toward secondhand/access models — simultaneously a threat to Retail and the thesis behind Nuuly.
