# Liking / Loving Tendency — Allbirds, Inc. (BIRD)

**Discipline:** Psychology  ·  **Intensity:** lens  ·  **As-of:** 2026-06-01

## What this model predicts

Munger's Liking/Loving Tendency: humans are wired to *love*, and that love distorts
cognition. The tendency operates in two directions here, and a disciplined analyst
must run both:

**(A) On the customer (the subject).** If a brand is genuinely *loved* — not merely
purchased — we should observe durable, self-reinforcing behavior that creates an
economic moat: (1) **pricing power** (customers tolerate price without defecting),
(2) **low churn / high repeat-purchase and referral** (the "ignore the faults of,
and comply with the wishes of, the loved object" distortion), (3) **resilience to
fashion and competitor entry** (love is sticky; preference is not), and (4)
**organic word-of-mouth** that lowers customer-acquisition cost over time. Liking
also has a dark twin: the same psychology that builds love makes the **withdrawal of
love violent** once disconfirming experience accumulates (a betrayed lover is harsher
than an indifferent one). So the model predicts that *conditional* love — love
contingent on an in-group signal or an unfulfilled promise — collapses faster than
no love at all.

**(B) On us, the analysts (the bias check).** Liking/Loving Tendency is one of
Munger's most dangerous *to the investor*. We are predisposed to like, and therefore
to over-trust: (1) **admired, likeable founders** (an All-White soccer captain and a
green-tech engineer with a Kickstarter-and-DiCaprio origin story), (2) a **morally
flattering narrative** (sustainability — loving a company because loving it makes
*us* feel good), and (3) a **fresh, exciting story** that lets us re-fall-in-love
after the first thesis broke (the AI pivot). The model predicts that wherever a
likeable story is present, our estimate of value will be biased *upward* and we
should deliberately discount it.

If the model applies *favorably*, BIRD should show pricing power, rising/steady
repeat rates, fashion resilience, and CAC that falls with scale. If it applies
*unfavorably*, we should see the opposite — and we should also catch ourselves being
seduced.

## Evidence

**Supporting (a real, if narrow, base of customer affection existed):**
- Allbirds earned genuinely strong **comfort** reviews and produced multi-pair repeat
  buyers ("customers owning four pairs," "most comfortable shoes they have ever worn")
  — early product love was real, not manufactured. (Trustpilot / Sitejabber /
  Thingtesting review aggregations, accessed 2026-06.)
- The brand won **high-profile organic advocacy** — Obama, Leonardo DiCaprio, Larry
  Page, Ashton Kutcher wore them; it became the de-facto Silicon Valley "uniform." That
  is the signature of a loved in-group object. (Wikipedia, "Allbirds"; NZ Herald
  2021-11-03.)
- The founding story is maximally *likeable*: ex-pro-footballer Tim Brown + renewable-
  materials engineer Joey Zwillinger, a $119K Kickstarter, a sustainability mission.
  (Inc. 2021; FashionNetwork; Wikipedia.) — Note: this is "supporting" only for the
  *bias-on-us* limb, not for customer economics.

**Disconfirming (the love was conditional, undurable, and is now gone):**
- **Durability destroyed the love.** Independent review bodies and post-mortems
  converge: shoes "wearing through the toe within 6–9 months," "soles delaminating after
  ~1 year," "notorious for often becoming unusable after only a year." A *self-described
  die-hard fan* who bought 5+ pairs documents "year after year… the same exact problems"
  and growing returns. (Chattermill, "What went wrong at Allbirds," accessed 2026-06;
  Trustpilot/Sitejabber.) Allbirds "leaned into sustainability… at the expense of
  durability and performance — factors that ultimately drive repeat purchase in
  footwear." (WWD 2026; GlobalData consumer panel via Wikipedia.)
- **The love was a fad/in-group signal, not a moat.** "Allbirds initially succeeded
  because its shoes were an exclusive 'in-group' signal for the Silicon Valley elite. As
  the brand expanded… and became ubiquitous, it lost its scarcity and cachet." Sales
  began declining by 2022 "as its brand was seen as part of 2010s Silicon Valley attire."
  (Wikipedia/GlobalData; WWD 2026; oliverfranklinwallis.substack.com, "The tragedy of
  Allbirds.")
- **No pricing power; the opposite.** Gross margin fell from low-50s% to **41% (FY2025)**
  and **collapsed to 27.8% in Q1-2026** on clearance/discount selling — customers would
  not pay the brand price, so the company chased volume with markdowns. (DOSSIER §2.1–2.2.)
- **Churn/defection is now structural, not anecdotal.** Revenue **−48.8% from the FY2022
  peak**, **−19.7% FY2025**, then **−30.5% YoY in Q1-2026**, "driven by falling DTC unit
  sales." Customers left for **On (+32–36% YoY)** and **Hoka ($2.23B, +23.6%)**. (DOSSIER
  §2.2, §4.) Customer love that produced moat-grade retention does not coexist with a
  one-third annual unit-volume decline.
- **Customer service eroded what affection remained** — reviews "frequently criticized"
  on returns/issue handling; "losing a lot of loyalty from customers disappointed in
  product quality." (Sitejabber; Chattermill.) This is the *betrayed-lover* dynamic the
  model predicts.
- **The economic verdict on the love:** the entire brand, IP, customer relationships and
  inventory were agreed to be sold for **$39M** — ~99% below the ~$4.1B IPO-era peak.
  (DOSSIER §1, §4.) Whatever love existed carried essentially **zero terminal franchise
  value**.

**Independent lens research run for this model (cited above):** Trustpilot, Sitejabber,
Thingtesting review corpora; Chattermill loyalty post-mortem; WWD "$4.1B to $39M";
GlobalData consumer-panel findings via Wikipedia; Inc./FashionNetwork/NZ Herald on the
founder narrative; Slate/NBC/InvestorPlace on the AI pivot framing.

## Reasoning

**On the customer (subject).** Apply the model honestly and it cuts *against* a moat.
There was real affection — comfort earned genuine multi-pair buyers, and the brand
briefly achieved the rare status of a *loved* in-group object. But the model
distinguishes love (sticky, fault-forgiving, durable) from preference (fashion-cyclical,
fault-sensitive). Allbirds' affection was the second kind wearing the costume of the
first. Two of Munger's own mechanisms explain the collapse: (1) the love was tethered to
an **in-group signal**, and Liking dynamics invert once the object becomes ubiquitous —
the signal value that *created* the love is destroyed by the success that spreads it;
(2) the product repeatedly **violated the implied promise** (durability), and the
fault-forgiving distortion of love is *finite* — once exhausted, the former lover
defects hard and warns others (negative word-of-mouth), which is exactly the review
record we see. The result is the textbook anti-moat: no pricing power (margins
collapsing into clearance), accelerating churn (−30% units), and rising effective CAC as
organic advocacy reversed into organic warning. **For the legacy business, the model's
signal is clearly negative.**

Crucially, the **forward** entity being valued is *not* the footwear brand at all — it
is NewBird AI, a pre-revenue GPU-leasing shell, and the Allbirds name is being *sold
away* to a licensing house (AXNY). So even the residual customer love does not accrue to
the security a buyer at $4.18 would own. The model's customer-love limb has **near-zero
relevance to the go-forward thesis** — there is no consumer, no brand, no loved object in
a GPUaaS stub. This collapses the *favorable* applicability of limb (A) to roughly nil.

**On us (bias check) — this is where the model earns its keep.** Liking/Loving Tendency
is most useful here as a **guard on our own reasoning**, and it fires loudly:
- *The original seduction.* Allbirds is a near-perfect specimen of a company that
  attracted capital because it was **likeable** rather than because it was **good** — an
  admirable founder story, celebrity halo, and a morally flattering sustainability
  mission that let investors feel virtuous. The $4.1B peak was, in part, the market
  pricing *being liked*. The 99% destruction is the bill for that distortion. The SEC
  even forced removal of the "first sustainable IPO" line — the narrative was doing work
  the facts couldn't. (Wikipedia.)
- *The fresh seduction (the live risk today).* The model predicts that after a broken
  love affair, humans seek a **new object to love** — and management has supplied one: a
  rebrand to "NewBird AI" riding the most exciting story in markets. The 4/15 pivot
  produced **+~582% intraday** (DOSSIER §7) — that is Liking/Loving Tendency repricing a
  *story*, not cash flows. A disciplined analyst must treat the AI narrative as the
  *same psychological trap in a new costume*, especially when the people telling it
  (footwear/apparel executives with **no cloud/GPU/data-center background**, DOSSIER §6)
  are not credible operators of it, and **no insider bought a share** into the spike
  (DOSSIER §6). The absence of skin-in-the-game is the tell that even insiders aren't
  "in love" — only the crowd is.

Net: the model both **explains the past** (a likeable, undurable love that built no moat
and destroyed capital) and **flags the present danger** (a new likeable story inviting us
to re-inflate value on emotion). Both readings point the same way: negative.

This is a Psychology lens, not a fatal-capable model (it is not incentives, inversion,
accounting, or margin-of-safety), so I record **no fatal flaw** in the technical sense —
though the *substance* it surfaces (a value-less brand + a seductive AI narrative atop a
death-spiral-funded shell) is corroborated by the fatal-capable lenses elsewhere.

## What would flip this read (disconfirmers)

- **Hard retention data showing love, not fad:** disclosed repeat-purchase rate, NPS, or
  cohort revenue retention that was *high and stable* through 2024–2025 (would reframe
  the decline as macro/competitive rather than lost love). *None disclosed; reviews and
  unit-decline argue the opposite.*
- **Pricing power evidence:** ability to hold full price without volume collapse, or
  rising gross margin. *Observed reverse (margin to 27.8%).*
- **Durability fixed and loyalty recovering** before divestiture (resolved quality
  complaints, improving review trajectory). *Not observed; problems persisted "year after
  year."*
- **Brand value realization contradicting the $39M sale** — e.g., a competing bid valuing
  the brand far higher because customer equity is durable. *The arms-length whole-company
  bid was $2.00–$2.25/sh; IP $30–40M — corroborating low brand value (DOSSIER §3).*
- **On the bias limb:** credible AI/cloud operators joining + insider open-market buying +
  contracted GPU backlog — which would mean the new story is *substance*, not just a fresh
  object to love. *None present as of 2026-06-01 (DOSSIER §6).*

**Bottom line:** Allbirds had real but *conditional* customer affection — comfort plus a
Silicon Valley in-group/ESG signal — which the model correctly predicts would be
fault-sensitive and fashion-cyclical, not a moat; chronic durability failures and lost
scarcity converted lovers into defectors (margins to 27.8%, units −30%, brand sold for
$39M ≈ 99% below peak). More importantly, the model fires hardest as a *check on us*: the
original $4.1B valuation priced a **likeable founder-and-sustainability story**, and the
market is now being invited to re-fall-in-love with an even more seductive **AI narrative**
on a pre-revenue shell run by non-experts with no insider buying. On both limbs the
tendency points the same way — be suspicious of the love, discount the story, and note
that the loved object (the brand) is being sold away from the equity entirely.
